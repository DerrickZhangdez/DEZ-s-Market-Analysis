# Daily Market Report — Monday, June 1, 2026

## Executive Summary

U.S. equities opened June on a mixed note as a dramatic surge in oil prices — WTI crude jumping ~7% to ~$94/barrel — rattled investor sentiment and offset enthusiasm from Nvidia's headline-grabbing Computex keynote in Taipei. The S&P 500 hugged the flatline (+0.06%), the Nasdaq edged marginally higher (+0.26%), while the Dow shed approximately 231 points (-0.45%) as energy costs weighed on consumer and industrial outlooks. Markets arrive at this juncture having just posted their ninth consecutive week of gains and the software sector's best month since 2001, meaning the near-term risk-reward skew is increasingly dependent on how the Iran situation evolves and whether Friday's jobs report shows continued labor-market resilience.

## Macro & Monetary Environment

### Federal Reserve Stance
The Federal Open Market Committee held the federal funds target rate at **3.50%–3.75%** at its April 29, 2026 meeting, and markets are pricing an approximately **98% probability** the Fed remains on hold at the June meeting as well. The central bank is caught in a difficult spot: the labor market remains robust, but energy-driven inflation is accelerating well ahead of the 2% target.

### Inflation Data
- **Headline PCE (March 2026):** +3.5% YoY (up from 2.8% in February)
- **Core PCE (March 2026):** +3.2% YoY (up from 2.9% in February)
- Economists at Nationwide now project headline inflation could peak near **4.5% this summer** — more than double the Fed's mandate — driven primarily by the oil price shock tied to the Iran conflict and Strait of Hormuz disruption risk

### Rate Cut/Hike Outlook
Bank of America has shelved its rate-cut forecast for 2026, now not expecting any cuts until **July–September 2027**. More strikingly, futures markets are now pricing approximately a **30% probability of a rate hike** by year-end — a dramatic repricing from the six-cut consensus that dominated Q4 2025.

## Geopolitical Factors

### U.S.–Iran Tensions — The Dominant Market Risk
The single largest market overhang this week is the deteriorating state of U.S.–Iran diplomatic engagement. Iran's semi-official Tasnim news agency reported Monday morning that Tehran has **suspended peace talks** with Washington, citing U.S. backing of Israeli operations in Lebanon and Gaza. This follows U.S. and Israeli strikes on Iranian targets in late February 2026, which briefly spiked Brent crude to **$117/barrel** in mid-April. WTI crude surged roughly **7% on Monday alone to ~$94/barrel**, and Brent rose approximately **6% to ~$97/barrel**.

The **Strait of Hormuz** — through which approximately 20% of global oil flows — remains a flashpoint. Any blockade or infrastructure damage could quickly reprice energy toward the $110–$117 range last seen in the peak of the crisis.

### Broader Geopolitical Backdrop
- Middle East tensions are feeding a **~$10/barrel geopolitical risk premium** into crude prices
- OPEC+ supply discipline and modest non-OPEC growth are providing limited offset
- The energy spike poses a secondary risk through **consumer confidence erosion** and potential demand destruction heading into summer driving season

## Sector Deep Dives

### Artificial Intelligence

**NVIDIA — Computex Keynote Steals the Show**
CEO Jensen Huang's keynote at **Computex 2026 in Taipei** was the week's most consequential tech event. Nvidia unveiled the **RTX Spark superchip**, designed to bring full AI agent functionality to laptops and desktops — marking NVIDIA's formal, aggressive entry into the PC processor market. This directly challenges Intel, AMD, Apple, and Qualcomm on their home turf. NVDA shares surged **+2–3% in premarket** trading on the news.

The announcement confirms that Nvidia's ambitions extend far beyond data centers. The **Vera Rubin platform** — its next-generation AI training and inference architecture — counts Anthropic, OpenAI, SpaceX, Meta, and all major hyperscalers among early adopters.

Despite the enthusiasm, context matters: NVDA is up only **~15% YTD**, just modestly ahead of the S&P 500's 10% gain, suggesting the stock has digested much of the near-term AI capex cycle excitement. The bigger story in 2026 has been the emergence of leaner AI plays: **Lumentum Holdings (LITE)** is up **121% YTD** as optical interconnect demand surges, and **Applied Materials (AMAT)** has jumped **67% YTD** on wafer fab equipment dominance.

**Meta Platforms** continues to monetize its AI investments aggressively: revenue grew **+33% YoY** in its most recent quarter, fueled by AI-enhanced ad targeting and rising impression counts. Meta remains one of the most visible examples of AI ROI conversion at hyperscale.

**Competitive Dynamics**
The AI agent race is intensifying at every layer of the stack: inference (NVDA, AMD), training (NVDA), edge/on-device (NVDA RTX Spark, Qualcomm), cloud orchestration (Azure, AWS, Google Cloud), and model/platform (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind).

### Hardware & Semiconductors

**TSMC (TSM)**
TSMC is arguably the most strategically positioned company in the AI infrastructure stack. Full-year 2025 revenue came in at **$122.9 billion (+31.6% YoY)**, and 2026 consensus points to another **~30% growth** year, driven by explosive demand for leading-edge N3/N2 node chips from Nvidia, Apple, AMD, and Broadcom. Trading at approximately **24x forward earnings**, TSMC is notably cheaper than both Nvidia and Broadcom despite similar growth rates. Shares are up **~42% YTD**.

**AMD**
AMD announced the **production ramp of its next-generation EPYC "Venice" server processor** on **TSMC's 2nm process** on May 21, 2026 — a significant competitive milestone against Intel's Xeon roadmap. AMD is gaining credibility in AI GPU inference with its MI-series, and enterprise server market share is trending upward.

**Broadcom (AVGO)**
With an AI-specific semiconductor revenue forecast exceeding **$30 billion in fiscal year 2026** and shares up **~41% YTD around $327**, Broadcom is benefiting from custom ASIC programs with Google, Meta, and Apple. The stock trades at a premium **~41x forward earnings** — the most expensive of the major chip players — but its diversified networking/custom silicon model justifies a higher multiple. **Broadcom reports earnings this Thursday**, making it the most important event for the semiconductor sector this week.

**Intel (INTC)**
Intel continues its difficult multi-year foundry pivot. The RTX Spark announcement from NVIDIA may incrementally undermine Intel's core laptop CPU market, adding to structural headwinds. Intel shares dipped slightly on the Computex news.

**ASML & Applied Materials**
Equipment makers remain beneficiaries of the global fab buildout supercycle. AMAT up 67% YTD reflects the multi-year commitment to advanced node capacity expansion across TSMC, Samsung, and Intel Foundry.

## Stock Picks — Valuation-Based

### 1. Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing (TSM)
**Rating: Strong Buy**

- **Thesis:** TSMC is the irreplaceable pick-and-shovel play on every major AI hardware program in the world. As the only credible manufacturer at N3/N2/N1.6 nodes, it has unassailable pricing power and is growing revenue ~30% annually while trading at a discount to its customers.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Forward P/E ~24x | P/S ~11x | EV/EBITDA ~15x | PEG ~0.8x
- **Growth Profile:** Revenue +31.6% YoY (2025); 2026 consensus ~+30% | EPS growth accelerating
- **Competitive Moat:** Physical manufacturing monopoly at leading-edge nodes; $100B+ capex advantage vs. peers; 50+ year customer relationships
- **Key Risk:** Taiwan geopolitical risk premium; China invasion scenario would be catastrophic for global tech supply chains
- **Entry Context:** At ~24x forward earnings vs. 41x for Broadcom and 35x+ for NVDA, TSM offers the most attractive risk-adjusted exposure to the AI capex cycle

---

### 2. Broadcom (AVGO)
**Rating: Buy (ahead of Thursday earnings)**

- **Thesis:** Broadcom's custom ASIC business (XPUs for Google TPUs, Meta MTIA, Apple inference chips) is a structural, multi-year secular winner that the market underappreciates relative to the GPU narrative. AI revenue exceeding $30B in FY2026 is already contracted.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Forward P/E ~41x | P/S ~18x | EV/EBITDA ~28x | PEG ~1.5x
- **Growth Profile:** AI segment growing ~45–50% YoY; total revenue +22% YoY | FCF margins >50%
- **Competitive Moat:** Deep co-development relationships make switching costs effectively infinite; networking silicon (Jericho) is the default in hyperscaler switching fabrics
- **Key Risk:** Concentration in top 3 customers (Google, Meta, Apple) creates revenue volatility risk; Thursday earnings report is binary near-term catalyst
- **Entry Context:** Premium valuation is earned; pullbacks to ~$300 would represent a compelling add

---

### 3. Micron Technology (MU)
**Rating: Strong Buy**

- **Thesis:** Micron is the cheapest way to own the HBM3E/HBM4 supercycle. Every Nvidia H100/H200/B200 GPU requires Micron (or SK Hynix) HBM. At ~12x forward earnings, the market is still pricing in semiconductor cycle risk — but the AI-driven DRAM upcycle is structurally different from prior cycles.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Forward P/E ~12.2x | P/S ~5x | EV/EBITDA ~8x | PEG ~0.4x
- **Growth Profile:** HBM revenue growing 3x+ YoY; total revenue inflecting sharply upward in FY2026
- **Competitive Moat:** Only U.S.-headquartered DRAM manufacturer; strategic national security significance; CHIPS Act beneficiary
- **Key Risk:** DRAM oversupply in commodity segments (PC/mobile DRAM) could pressure blended ASPs; China export restrictions remain a wildcard
- **Entry Context:** At 12x forward P/E with HBM monetization just beginning, Micron offers the clearest "value + growth" combination in semis

---

### 4. Qualcomm (QCOM)
**Rating: Buy**

- **Thesis:** Qualcomm is being dramatically undervalued on the assumption that its smartphone SoC business will plateau. In reality, its Snapdragon X Elite chip is the leading on-device AI processor for Windows PCs, and its automotive/IoT design wins are building a durable, high-margin revenue diversification story.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Forward P/E ~19.5x | P/S ~5.5x | EV/EBITDA ~13x | PEG ~1.2x
- **Growth Profile:** Automotive design wins up 20%+ YoY; AI PC TAM expanding; licensing revenue stable
- **Competitive Moat:** ARM ISA leadership, proprietary Oryon CPU cores, decades of wireless modem IP
- **Key Risk:** Apple's potential in-house modem success (rumored for 2027 iPhone) would remove its largest single licensing customer
- **Entry Context:** At ~19.5x earnings with multiple secular growth vectors, QCOM is the most undervalued large-cap AI chip play that isn't purely a data center story

---

### 5. ServiceNow (NOW)
**Rating: Accumulate**

- **Thesis:** ServiceNow is emerging as the operating system for enterprise AI orchestration. Its agentic AI workflows (announced at Knowledge 2026 conference) are seeing strong enterprise attach rates, and CIO surveys consistently rank it as the #1 platform for AI ROI in IT operations.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Forward P/E ~55x | P/S ~22x | EV/EBITDA ~45x | PEG ~2.2x
- **Growth Profile:** cRPO growth ~22% YoY; NRR >125%; FCF margins expanding
- **Competitive Moat:** Deep enterprise workflow lock-in; average Fortune 500 customer uses 8+ product lines; switching cost is measured in years
- **Key Risk:** Premium multiple leaves little room for guidance disappointment; macro slowdown could cause enterprise IT budget freezes
- **Entry Context:** Accumulate on pullbacks; the valuation is stretched near-term but the compounding story over 3–5 years is intact

---

### 6. Snowflake (SNOW)
**Rating: Accumulate (after earnings pop)**

- **Thesis:** The 36% earnings-day surge validated Snowflake's AI-native data platform pivot. Its Cortex AI layer, document AI, and Snowpark Container Services are converting the platform from a pure warehouse to an AI data hub — directly refuting the "SaaSpocalypse" narrative.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Forward P/S ~17x | EV/Revenue ~16x | NTM P/FCF ~60x | PEG ~3.0x (growth-adjusted)
- **Growth Profile:** Product revenue +26% YoY (beat estimates); RPO +34% YoY; consumption model rebounding
- **Competitive Moat:** Data gravity (moving petabytes is prohibitively expensive); Marketplace ecosystem; AI/ML workload portability across clouds
- **Key Risk:** AWS Redshift, Google BigQuery, and Microsoft Fabric all competing for the same budget with deeply discounted pricing
- **Entry Context:** Avoid chasing the 40%+ pop; build a position on a 10–15% pullback from current levels

## Risk Factors

Ranked by severity and near-term probability:

1. **Iran/Oil Price Escalation (HIGH)** — Iran's suspension of U.S. peace talks and Monday's 7% WTI spike represents the clearest and most immediate threat to the bull case. If Strait of Hormuz flows are disrupted, oil could re-test $110–$117, forcing the Fed into a more hawkish posture and crushing consumer discretionary.

2. **Fed Rate Hike Repricing (HIGH)** — With PCE at 3.5% and energy prices re-accelerating, the ~30% implied probability of a 2026 rate hike is rising. Any upside surprise in Friday's NFP (strong wage growth) or next week's CPI print could materially re-price rate expectations and pressure equity multiples.

3. **Nonfarm Payrolls — Friday (MEDIUM-HIGH)** — The May jobs report is the week's most consequential scheduled data release. A hot number (~225K+ with strong wages) could trigger the rate-hike repricing outlined above. A soft number (~100K) would raise recession concerns.

4. **Broadcom Earnings Thursday (MEDIUM)** — AVGO is a real-time report card for AI capex commitments from hyperscalers. Any indication that Google, Meta, or Apple is slowing custom ASIC spending would reprice the entire AI infrastructure trade sharply.

5. **Software Sector Valuation Reversion (MEDIUM)** — After the best SaaS month since 2001, the IGV ETF is pricing considerable AI-monetization optimism. If Microsoft's Build 2026 conference disappoints or Salesforce's Agentforce metrics remain weak, the momentum reversal could be swift.

## Bottom Line

June opens with a familiar tension: the AI infrastructure story is as compelling as ever — validated freshly by Snowflake's earnings and NVIDIA's Computex showcase — but a genuine macro shock in the form of oil prices is now running in the opposite direction, threatening both inflation and consumer spending at the worst possible time. For investors with a 12-month horizon, the core thesis remains intact: own TSMC, Micron, and ServiceNow as the highest-conviction, best-value expressions of the AI buildout. In the near term, Friday's jobs data and Thursday's Broadcom earnings are the two pivots around which this week's price action will rotate.
