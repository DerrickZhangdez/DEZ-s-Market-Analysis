## Executive Summary

U.S. equities staged a broad, tech-led rally on Thursday as two powerful catalysts converged: the signing of a preliminary U.S.-Iran interim peace deal (including the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz) and a late-day surge in semiconductor stocks sparked by President Trump's announcement of an Intel-Apple domestic chip-manufacturing partnership. The Nasdaq Composite surged nearly 2% while the S&P 500 added over 1%, both bouncing sharply from the Fed-induced sell-off earlier in the week. The rally came despite a hawkish pivot from new Federal Reserve Chairman Kevin Warsh, who held rates steady at 3.5%–3.75% but removed cut-forward guidance and signaled the door to further hikes is open. With markets closed Friday for Juneteenth, investors head into the weekend balancing geopolitical optimism against renewed inflation anxiety and a Federal Reserve that is no longer a tailwind.

## Macro & Monetary Environment

### Federal Reserve — June 16–17 FOMC Meeting
New Federal Reserve Chairman **Kevin Warsh** chaired his first FOMC meeting and delivered a hawkish-leaning hold:

- **Decision:** Benchmark overnight rate held steady at **3.50%–3.75%** (unanimous vote)
- **Inflation Outlook:** The Fed raised its 2026 headline inflation forecast to **3.6%** and core to **3.3%** — a three-year high for the latter
- **Critical Shift in Guidance:** The FOMC removed the forward-looking language that had indicated a "bias toward future cuts," effectively closing the door on near-term easing
- **Rate Hike Odds:** Markets now price approximately a **40% probability** of a 25 bps hike at the December 2026 meeting
- **Cut Timeline:** The new dot plot pushes any rate reductions into **2027–2028**, a significant hawkish repricing from the prior "one cut in 2026" baseline

### Inflation Backdrop
Elevated inflation is being driven primarily by energy price pass-through from the Iran conflict, tariff pressures on imported goods, and residual services stickiness. Warsh's public comments emphasize that the Fed will not allow elevated energy prices to become entrenched in core inflation expectations — a signal that the hiking bias is real, not rhetorical.

### What to Watch This Week (June 23 onward)
- **Monday, June 23:** Markets reopen post-Juneteenth; Fed speakers likely to elaborate on Warsh's hawkish pivot
- **Tuesday–Wednesday:** Housing starts, existing home sales, PMI flash data
- **Thursday:** Jobless claims, GDP revision (Q1 final), PCE deflator preview
- **Friday:** Personal income/spending + PCE Price Index — the Fed's preferred inflation measure; any upside surprise here will cement December hike expectations

## Geopolitical Factors

### U.S.-Iran Interim Peace Deal — Primary Market Driver
The most consequential macro development of the week was the signing of a preliminary U.S.-Iran peace agreement. The deal includes:

- **Reopening of the Strait of Hormuz**, through which ~21% of global oil supply transits
- An immediate reduction in naval tensions between U.S. and Iranian forces
- A framework for continued diplomatic engagement

**Market Impact:** Energy prices pulled back sharply on the news as the geopolitical risk premium embedded in crude oil began to unwind. This is enormously positive for global supply chains and helps ease the inflationary pressure the Fed has been battling. The VIX's 11% single-day collapse is a direct reflection of this tail-risk removal.

### Oil Market Tension: Peace vs. Structural Oversupply
Oil markets face a peculiar dynamic heading into the second half of 2026:

- **Bearish structural:** The IEA has flagged a projected **surplus of nearly 4 million barrels per day** for 2026, driven by OPEC+ production increases and slowing demand growth from China
- **Bullish geopolitical:** War premium has kept Brent crude above fundamentally warranted levels; J.P. Morgan's base case is ~**$60/bbl Brent** for 2026 as the risk premium fades
- **Net read:** Energy stocks face headwinds as the Iran deal materializes; crude likely drifts toward the $55–65 range absent new supply disruptions

### Trade & Tariffs
The broader tariff regime remains in place. The Intel-Apple domestic manufacturing announcement signals the administration's continued push for semiconductor reshoring, which has policy and subsidy implications for U.S.-listed chip companies (INTC, QCOM, TXN beneficiaries; TSM ADR a more complex read).

## Sector Deep Dives

### Artificial Intelligence

The AI infrastructure thesis remains the dominant investment narrative of 2026. The story has meaningfully broadened from "buy NVIDIA only" to a multi-layer opportunity across hardware, cloud, software, and applications.

**NVIDIA (NVDA):** Shares closed at approximately **$210.69** on June 18, having surged ~8% earlier in the week after Citi analysts raised price targets, citing projections that global chip manufacturing spending could grow from $145 billion in 2026 to nearly **$250 billion by 2028** — the majority driven by AI infrastructure. Citi's thesis: NVIDIA's GPU monopoly on AI training and inference remains intact through at least 2027.

**Microsoft (MSFT):** Continues to monetize AI investment through Azure OpenAI capacity and Copilot enterprise subscriptions. The company is one of the largest buyers of NVIDIA GPUs globally and remains the clearest enterprise AI distribution platform.

**Alphabet (GOOGL):** Competing aggressively in the AI cloud race through Google Cloud's TPU infrastructure and the Gemini model family. Historically undervalued relative to Microsoft on AI narrative despite comparable (or superior) technical capabilities.

**Meta Platforms (META):** Heavy AI capital expenditure is translating into advertising ROI improvements via Advantage+ AI targeting and Llama model ecosystem development. Big Tech AI is no longer purely a cost center — it is a revenue driver at Meta.

**Competitive Dynamics:** The market is increasingly differentiating between AI enablers (NVDA, TSMC, ASML), AI platforms (MSFT, GOOGL, META, AMZN), and AI applications (ServiceNow, Salesforce, Palantir). The first two tiers remain the most defensible as the application layer faces disruption risk from model commoditization.

### Hardware & Semiconductors

This was the standout sector of Thursday's session.

**Intel (INTC):** The single biggest stock story of the week. Shares surged **~10.5% to $133.82** after President Trump publicly confirmed a preliminary partnership with Apple to design and manufacture chips domestically. Key context:
- Wall Street Journal first reported the preliminary Intel-Apple discussions in early May 2026
- Neither Apple nor Intel has issued an official press release confirming the deal
- The U.S. government holds a 10% stake in Intel — a conflict of interest worth monitoring
- Still, the strategic signal is powerful: if Apple diversifies even 5–10% of its chip design to Intel foundry, it provides Intel a marquee anchor customer for its turnaround

**TSMC (TSM):** Shares rose **+4.72% to $452.54**, driven by continued enthusiasm for AI chip demand and the company's May 2026 revenue report showing **30% YoY growth**. Early 2026 saw 35.1% YoY revenue growth overall. TSMC remains the world's irreplaceable leading-edge chip manufacturer. 13 analyst buy ratings, 2 holds, zero sells. At ~24x forward earnings it is the most attractively valued of the major AI chip names.

**NVIDIA (NVDA):** After a strong mid-week bounce, shares settled at ~$210.69. Forward estimates continue to be revised upward as hyperscaler capex remains elevated.

**AMD:** Analysts maintain a consensus Buy with an average 12-month price target implying ~39% upside from current levels. AMD is gaining share in AI inference workloads as customers seek GPU alternatives.

**Broadcom (AVGO):** Trading around **$327/share**, +41% YTD. At ~41x forward earnings it is the most expensive of the major chip names but benefits from both AI infrastructure (custom ASICs for hyperscalers) and infrastructure software (VMware integration). Its cautious AI chip commentary in early June triggered a temporary sector-wide sell-off — a reminder that guidance warnings carry outsized weight.

**ASML / AMAT:** Semiconductor equipment makers remain leveraged to the multi-year fab build-out cycle. Any diplomatic progress with Taiwan (or deterioration) is a key exogenous risk to monitor.

### Software & SaaS

The SaaS sector has been the most volatile in 2026, whipsawed between "AI kills software" fears and "AI accelerates software adoption" optimism.

**ServiceNow (NOW):** One of the cleanest enterprise AI beneficiaries. The company's Now Assist agentic AI platform is driving measurable ROI for customers, which is accelerating renewal and upsell cycles. Fell on Fed rate concerns Thursday as its premium multiple (40–45x forward earnings) makes it sensitive to rate re-pricing. This creates a potential accumulation opportunity for long-term holders.

**Oracle (ORCL):** Added +6% earlier in the week on the AI data infrastructure rally. Oracle's cloud infrastructure (OCI) is winning significant AI training workload contracts, particularly from companies unable to secure capacity from AWS/Azure/GCP. Mizuho rates Oracle a top enterprise software pick for 2026.

**Salesforce (CRM):** The laggard in the recent software rally. Shares slipped ~4.1% on lackluster forward guidance despite Agentforce adoption metrics improving. The market is skeptical that Agentforce (Salesforce's AI agent platform) can move the revenue needle fast enough to justify its premium. Mizuho remains constructive on the long-term thesis.

**Snowflake (SNOW):** Surged 36% in the past quarter on renewed AI data infrastructure enthusiasm. The data cloud remains essential for AI workflows, and Snowflake's integration with major AI model providers (Anthropic, OpenAI) positions it as the "data layer" for enterprise AI. Valuation remains stretched (~25–30x forward revenue) but growth is reaccelerating.

**SAP:** European enterprise software giant continuing its AI integration into its ERP ecosystem. Less headline-grabbing than U.S. names but increasingly relevant for multinational enterprises.

## Risk Factors

Ranked by severity heading into the week of June 23:

1. **🔴 Federal Reserve Hawkish Pivot (HIGH):** Warsh's removal of cut-forward guidance and the 40% market-implied probability of a December hike represents a fundamental repricing of the interest rate environment. Duration-sensitive growth stocks (software, SaaS, unprofitable tech) face multiple compression if economic data comes in hot. Any upside surprise in Friday's PCE print could accelerate this risk.

2. **🟠 Iran Peace Deal Fragility (HIGH-MEDIUM):** The interim U.S.-Iran agreement is preliminary and unconfirmed by either party. If the deal collapses — or if Iran resumes blockade actions in the Strait of Hormuz — oil prices could spike 15–20% rapidly, reigniting inflation fears and triggering a sharp market sell-off.

3. **🟠 Semiconductor Valuation & Guidance Risk (MEDIUM):** After a spectacular run YTD, semis are pricing near-perfection. Broadcom's cautious AI chip commentary in early June triggered a sector-wide sell-off. Any guidance miss from a major name (NVDA, AVGO, AMD) could cascade broadly given the sector's elevated weight in major indices.

4. **🟡 Intel-Apple Deal Uncertainty (MEDIUM):** The 10.5% surge in INTC is based on an unconfirmed presidential announcement with no official company statements. If Apple clarifies the deal is smaller or further out than implied, Intel gives back the gains and creates noise across the broader chip sector.

5. **🟡 Inflation Re-Acceleration / Stagflation (MEDIUM):** If energy prices stabilize (not fall) and tariff pass-through continues, CPI and PCE readings could remain stubbornly above 3%, trapping the Fed between a slowing economy and persistent inflation — the worst-case stagflationary scenario for equities.

---

## Bottom Line

Thursday's rally was genuine and fundamentally driven — peace-deal optimism removed a meaningful macro tail risk while the semiconductor sector received fresh catalyst from the Intel-Apple announcement. However, the Federal Reserve's hawkish pivot is a structural headwind that markets cannot ignore: rate cuts are no longer in the 2026 forecast, and there is now a non-trivial probability of further hikes. For investors, the playbook is to stay long AI infrastructure quality (TSMC, Micron, NVIDIA) with selective accumulation of undervalued software names (Cisco, Alphabet) on rate-driven pullbacks. Avoid chasing momentum in names that have already priced in perfection. The Iran deal is the wildcard — if it holds, it structurally improves the macro backdrop for the rest of 2026; if it fractures, expect a swift and painful reversal in risk assets.
