## Geopolitical Factors

### U.S.–Iran Conflict & Strait of Hormuz Crisis (Dominant Structural Risk)

The single largest geopolitical driver of 2026 markets remains the U.S.–Iran conflict that escalated in February 2026 and Iran's de facto closure of the Strait of Hormuz beginning **March 4, 2026**. The International Energy Agency called this the **greatest global energy security challenge in history**:

- **~25–30% of global crude oil** and **~20% of global LNG** transit the Strait.
- The disruption has produced multi-year-high energy prices, forcing airlines, manufacturers, and utilities to absorb or pass on unprecedented input cost increases.
- **Europe is acutely exposed:** Gas storage levels sat at approximately 38% of capacity as of late May 2026, far below seasonal norms, primarily due to suspended Qatari LNG shipments.
- **Food prices:** Roughly one-third of global fertilizer shipments transit Hormuz, raising concerns about agricultural input costs and food inflation globally.

**Monday's easing signal:** Geopolitical chatter on June 29 suggested partial diplomatic back-channels opening, which contributed to the Monday rally. However, no formal ceasefire or shipping corridor agreement has been reached. Any escalation remains a severe tail risk.

### Other Geopolitical Factors
- **U.S. trade policy:** Tariff discussions with major trading partners continue in the background; no material new developments on June 30.
- **AI regulation:** The White House's June 26 announcement restricting GPT-5.6 access to ~20 government-approved partners paradoxically *helped* enterprise software stocks by signaling AI agents won't rapidly displace existing SaaS workflows.

## Sector Deep Dives

### Today's Spotlight: Enterprise Software & SaaS

**The Big Picture: Mispriced by Fear, Not Fundamentals**

The SaaS sector has suffered a brutal derate over the past 18 months. The narrative has been uniform: *AI will commoditize SaaS, agents will replace seats, vertical software moats will erode.* The sell-off has been indiscriminate — quality names with 15–25% revenue growth, 30–40% free cash flow margins, and >110% net revenue retention have been marked down 30–55% from their peaks.

The reality is more nuanced. The White House's GPT-5.6 restrictions (June 26) provided an unexpected validation: enterprise AI deployment is *not* a simple wholesale replacement of existing software stacks. Compliance, governance, auditability, data sovereignty, and workflow integration requirements mean that **enterprises still need the orchestration layers, data platforms, and vertical workflows that SaaS incumbents provide.** AI may be additive — driving higher ARPU via AI tiers — rather than substitutive.

**Public SaaS Valuation Context (June 2026):**
- Median NTM revenue multiple for public SaaS companies has compressed to approximately **5–7x** from a peak of 15–20x in 2021.
- The Morningstar software sector has broadly underperformed the S&P 500 YTD by 8–12 percentage points.
- Quality SaaS companies with >$1B ARR, >115% NRR, and positive FCF are now trading near 5-year valuation trough levels on P/S and EV/EBITDA — a historically durable setup for forward returns.

**Key SaaS Moves This Week:**
- **Guidewire Software (GWRE):** Q3 FY2026 earnings beat — 27% revenue growth, 35% subscription/support growth, ARR at $1.15B (+19% YoY). Management raised full-year FY26 guidance to $1.46–$1.47B. Paradoxically, the stock *slid* post-earnings on near-term margin scrutiny — creating a buying opportunity in an operationally healthy name. Analysts now target $207 on average, vs. a current price near $121.
- **HubSpot (HUBS):** Down 50%+ from all-time highs, but delivered Q1 2026 revenue growth of 23% YoY with 10,800 net new customers added. Trading at 2.0x next year's revenue — near the lowest valuation in its public history.
- **Veeva Systems (VEEV):** FY2026 revenues of $3.195B (+16% YoY) with subscription revenues growing 17%. Vault CRM has 150+ live customers. Trading at ~16x NTM P/E — remarkably inexpensive for a vertical SaaS compounder with 77% GAAP gross margins.
- **Sprinklr (CXM):** Sprinklr Service ARR grew 50% YoY in FY26, though the company remains sub-scale for large institutional portfolios.

**The AI Opportunity Within SaaS:**
Rather than the "AI destroys SaaS" narrative, the more accurate framing is that AI is creating **two classes of SaaS companies:**
1. **AI-enhanced incumbents** — companies whose data moats and workflow integrations make them the natural layer for AI deployment (Veeva, Guidewire, Paycom, Jack Henry).
2. **AI-commoditized SaaS** — companies whose products can be replicated by general-purpose AI, typically in horizontal, undifferentiated workflows.

The differentiation is the key to picking in this sector today.

### 1. HubSpot (HUBS) — Marketing & CRM SaaS
- **Ticker & Company:** HUBS — HubSpot, Inc.

AI disruption fears have been dramatically overpriced into the stock; HubSpot's SMB and mid-market customers have *not* abandoned the platform. - **Competitive Moat:** Network effects within the SMB and mid-market CRM/marketing stack; all-in-one platform creates stickiness across sales, marketing, service, and ops; >10 years of customer behavioral data advantage

## Risk Factors

Ranked by market severity:

1. **Hormuz/Iran Escalation Spike (Extreme Tail Risk):** Any material escalation — naval engagement, blockade tightening, retaliatory strikes on Saudi/UAE infrastructure — could send energy prices dramatically higher and trigger a risk-off flight that would overwhelm any equity thesis. PCE inflation would re-accelerate; the Fed would face a "hike into recession" scenario. Every portfolio's biggest risk is this.

2. **Fed Surprises with Pre-Emptive Rate Hike (Moderate Probability, High Impact):** With 9 of 18 FOMC members already projecting a hike before year-end, an upside CPI or PCE print (e.g., July 14–15 reports) could force the Fed's hand. A surprise 25–50bps hike would compress tech valuations by 5–10% on the day and reset rate-sensitive SaaS multiples lower.

3. **Hyperscaler AI CapEx Rationalization (Medium-Term Structural Risk):** The aggregate AI capex vs. free cash flow mismatch (70% vs. 23% growth rates) suggests hyperscalers face an FCF negative inflection in H2 2026. Any announcement of CapEx cuts would send NVIDIA, AMD, TSMC, and AI infrastructure names down sharply, with contagion into broader tech.

4. **European Energy Crisis Deepening (Ongoing Risk):** With European gas storage at 38% of seasonal capacity, a colder-than-expected autumn/winter 2026–2027 or a further disruption to remaining LNG supply routes could cause severe European energy rationing. This has negative knock-on effects for U.S. multinationals with European exposure and global industrial demand.

5. **AI Regulatory Landscape Uncertainty (Emerging Risk):** The White House's GPT-5.6 restrictions are an early signal of a broader AI governance ramp. More aggressive regulation — particularly around agentic AI, autonomous decision-making in financial services, or healthcare AI — could create compliance burdens and slow adoption curves for AI-forward software companies.

## Bottom Line

Today's market sits at a compelling inflection point: the macro environment is challenging (rates higher for longer, energy shock, geopolitical uncertainty), yet valuations in quality software and SaaS have corrected to levels that historically represent durable entry points. The Dow's record close above 52,000 and Alphabet's index debut are symbolic milestones, but the real opportunity lies in the unloved, high-quality mid-cap SaaS names that have been indiscriminately sold on AI disruption fears — businesses like HubSpot, Veeva, Guidewire, and Paycom, which continue to compound free cash flow while trading near multi-year valuation lows. For long-term investors with a 12–18 month horizon, the Enterprise Software & SaaS sector deserves meaningful allocation at these prices, with position sizing calibrated to the Hormuz/Fed tail risks that remain very much alive.
