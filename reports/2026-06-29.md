# Daily Market Report — Monday, June 29, 2026

## Executive Summary

U.S. equity futures opened the week sharply higher on Monday after the United States and Iran agreed to "stand down for now," easing fears of a sustained military escalation that had rattled markets last week. Nasdaq 100 futures climbed 1.2%, S&P 500 futures gained 0.8%, and Dow futures added roughly 0.4%, partially reversing the prior week's steep losses (S&P 500 –1.95%, Nasdaq –4.6%). Meanwhile, AI sector sentiment remains fragile following a wave of negative headlines around hyperscaler free cash flow turning negative and an OpenAI IPO delay — but enterprise software names staged a sharp recovery late last week on GPT-5.6 regulatory news.

## Macro & Monetary Environment

**Fed Stance — On Hold, Leaning Hawkish**

The Federal Reserve held the federal funds rate steady at **3.50%–3.75%** at its June 17 FOMC meeting, in line with consensus. New Chair Kevin Warsh reaffirmed the Committee's commitment to price stability. The updated dot plot was notably hawkish: the median projection now calls for **no cuts in 2026**, with the median end-of-year rate lifted to 3.8% — implying a hike remains on the table. Rate reductions have been pushed into 2027–2028.

**Inflation — Stubbornly Elevated**

- **PCE inflation for 2026 revised to 3.6%** (from prior 2.7% estimate), driven largely by energy supply shocks from the Hormuz closure.
- 2027 PCE also revised to 3.3%, suggesting the Fed views the inflationary impulse as persistent.
- Core PCE (ex-food/energy) is presumably more contained, but headline is dominating policy.

**Key Watch Items This Week:**
- ISM Manufacturing (Monday), JOLTS job openings (Tuesday), ADP private payrolls (Wednesday)
- Friday's **June Non-Farm Payrolls** is the marquee release — any softness in labor could complicate the Fed's hawkish pivot
- Fed speakers: Several officials scheduled mid-week; watch for tone on 2026 hike probability

## Geopolitical Factors

**U.S.–Iran Conflict & Hormuz Strait Crisis (Dominant Risk)**

The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since late February 2026 (>3 months), with shipping traffic near zero following the onset of military action on February 28. Key facts:

- Middle East oil producers have cut output by **>11 million barrels/day** vs. pre-conflict levels
- Brent crude averaged **$107/bbl in May**, declining $10/bbl from April — the first monthly decline since December 2025
- The EIA baseline scenario assumes Hormuz reopens in Q3 2026, with traffic normalizing by early 2027
- Today's "stand-down" announcement sent oil futures lower in early trading, though markets remain skeptical of a durable resolution
- Global oil demand is now forecast to **decline by 1.1 million bpd** over the course of 2026 due to high prices and government conservation initiatives

**Risk Assessment:** The de-escalation news is market-positive but fragile. Any re-escalation could immediately spike oil back above $110–120/bbl, rekindling inflation fears and pushing the Fed further hawkish. This remains the single largest tail risk in the market.

**Other Geopolitical Watch Items:**
- U.S.–China technology export controls (ongoing; semiconductor sector sensitivity)
- Global supply chain re-routing costs elevated as tankers use Cape of Good Hope route (+$2–3/bbl freight premium)

## Sector Deep Dives

### Artificial Intelligence

The AI sector is undergoing a significant correction and investor reckoning. The "Magnificent 7" are all in correction territory (–10%+ from highs), with Microsoft and Meta having fallen into bear market territory (–20%+ from peaks).

**Key Themes:**
- **CapEx vs. Cash Flow Crisis:** AI capital expenditure is growing at ~70% annually while hyperscaler free cash flow is growing at only ~23%. The aggregate implies hyperscaler FCF turns **negative by Q3 2026** — a structural concern that has weighed heavily on the group
- **Meta:** Down 5–7% last week after an FT report that the company is weighing a multi-billion-dollar equity raise to fund 2026 capex of **$125–145 billion**. Investors are balking at dilution
- **NVIDIA (NVDA):** Fell 4.15% last week but Morningstar estimates the stock is **~25% undervalued** relative to a $280 fair value. As the world's largest company by market cap, NVIDIA remains the AI infrastructure backbone
- **OpenAI IPO Delay:** OpenAI is reportedly considering delaying its IPO amid market volatility, unable to secure its desired $1 trillion valuation — another sentiment headwind for the sector
- **June 26 Catalyst — GPT-5.6 Lockdown:** The White House announced restrictions limiting GPT-5.6 access to ~20 government-approved partners, which paradoxically *boosted* enterprise software stocks by signaling that AI agents won't immediately displace existing SaaS workflows

**Near-Term Outlook:** Today's geopolitical relief rally is helping AI names bounce. However, the fundamental debate — "when does AI capex translate to earnings?" — will continue to cap recoveries until hyperscalers show credible ROI at scale. The next major catalyst will be Q2 earnings season (mid-July).

### Hardware & Semiconductors

**TSMC (TSM):** The standout performer in semis — **+46% YTD** as of late June 2026. Strong alliances with NVIDIA, AMD, Apple, and Broadcom drive robust 2026 wafer demand. Trades at ~24x forward earnings — cheaper than both NVIDIA and Broadcom on a relative basis despite best-in-class growth. The Hormuz crisis has not materially impacted TSMC given Taiwan's non-Middle East supply chain.

**NVIDIA (NVDA):** Tumbled 4.15% last week during the broader AI selloff. Remains the critical pick-and-shovel play for AI infrastructure. Morningstar fair value ~$280 (stock trading ~25% below that estimate). B200/GB200 Blackwell ramp is proceeding; H20 China export restrictions remain a ~$5–10B annual headwind.

**AMD:** YTD gains despite a sharp sector selloff on June 5. MI300X GPU gaining enterprise traction; datacenter revenue growing. PEG ratio of ~0.8x makes it one of the more attractively valued names in semis. Data center AI chip revenue on track to exceed $10B in 2026.

**Broadcom (AVGO):** +14.1% YTD but experienced a sharp –14% correction on June 4 after Q3 AI chip guidance of $16B missed the $17.2B consensus. Long-term AI ASIC opportunity (custom chips for hyperscalers) remains intact. PEG ~0.9x.

**Intel (INTC):** YTD gains but faces structural challenges. 18A foundry process is the key watch item — success could be transformative; failure could accelerate the decline. Trading at a significant discount to peers.

**Micron (MU):** Forward P/E of ~12x (vs. sector ~17x). Long-term supply agreements with NVIDIA, AMD, and Intel position Micron to capture HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) demand for AI GPU clusters. One of the most undervalued names relative to AI exposure.

**Applied Materials (AMAT):** Forward P/E ~26.6x (vs. sector ~34.5x). Semiconductor equipment demand tied to TSMC and Samsung capex cycles — both remain robust.

### Software & SaaS

The enterprise software sector staged a dramatic reversal last week after months of AI disruption fears. The June 26 White House announcement limiting GPT-5.6 distribution to approved partners was interpreted as validation that traditional SaaS workflows won't be disrupted imminently.

**ServiceNow (NOW):** +9.9% on June 26 to $98.34, though still **–36% YTD**. The company is positioning its platform as the AI workflow orchestration layer for enterprises, which is resonating with customers. At current levels, the YTD drawdown may be pricing in excessive disruption risk.

**Salesforce (CRM):** +5.4% on June 26 to $158.37, but still **–40% YTD**. Agentforce (its AI agent platform) is gaining enterprise traction. At –40% YTD, the risk/reward is increasingly compelling for patient investors.

**Snowflake (SNOW):** Surged ~36% in late May 2026 on AI data platform momentum, with the rally refreshed again on June 26 (+9.6%). The company has repositioned as critical AI data infrastructure — a compelling recovery story.

**Oracle (ORCL):** Fell 2.5% on June 26, diverging from the software rally due to its cloud infrastructure ties to OpenAI (a perceived negative given the GPT-5.6 lockdown). However, Oracle's cloud infrastructure (OCI) remains one of the fastest-growing hyperscaler alternatives. The dip may be a buying opportunity.

**Workday (WDAY):** +9.2% on June 26. AI-enhanced HR/Finance workflows driving renewal and expansion.

## Stock Picks — Valuation-Based

### 1. Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing (TSM)
- **Thesis:** TSMC is the indispensable infrastructure layer for every AI chip — NVIDIA, AMD, Apple, and Broadcom all depend on TSMC's leading-edge nodes (N3, N2). The Hormuz crisis hasn't impacted its supply chain. At 24x forward earnings, it's cheaper than both of its key customers on a relative basis, yet growing faster.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Forward P/E ~24x; P/S ~10x; EV/EBITDA ~15x; PEG ~1.2x
- **Growth Profile:** Revenue +30–35% YoY in 2026; EPS growing faster due to operating leverage on CoWoS/advanced packaging capacity
- **Competitive Moat:** 2–3 node-generation lead over Samsung; only foundry capable of manufacturing leading-edge AI chips at volume
- **Key Risk:** Taiwan geopolitical risk (PRC); potential U.S./Taiwan trade friction; customer concentration (NVIDIA ~20%+ of revenue)
- **Rating:** ⭐ **Strong Buy**

---

### 2. Micron Technology (MU)
- **Thesis:** HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) demand is exploding with AI GPU deployments. Micron has locked in supply agreements with NVIDIA, AMD, and Intel. At 12x forward earnings — nearly half the sector multiple — the market is pricing in a cyclical downturn that may not materialize given structural AI demand.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Forward P/E ~12x; P/S ~3.5x; EV/EBITDA ~8x; PEG ~0.5x
- **Growth Profile:** Revenue +25–30% YoY in 2026; HBM segment growing 100%+ from base; margin expansion underway
- **Competitive Moat:** HBM3E technology competitive with SK Hynix; DRAM and NAND duopoly dynamics; strategic customer lock-in
- **Key Risk:** Memory pricing is cyclical and can turn quickly; Samsung competitive response in HBM; macro demand softening
- **Rating:** ⭐ **Strong Buy**

---

### 3. Broadcom (AVGO)
- **Thesis:** Broadcom's custom AI ASIC business (designing chips for Google TPUs, Meta's MTIA) is a multi-year secular growth opportunity that the market has underpriced since the June 4 guidance miss. With a PEG of ~0.9x, the stock is trading below the Lynch fair-value threshold despite being the dominant custom silicon supplier for the three largest AI spenders.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Forward P/E ~28x; P/S ~12x; EV/EBITDA ~20x; PEG ~0.9x
- **Growth Profile:** AI semiconductor revenue growing 40%+ YoY; VMware integration driving software revenue mix higher (boosting margins)
- **Competitive Moat:** Custom ASIC design relationships are sticky (multi-year design cycles); networking silicon (Tomahawk/Jericho) ~70% market share; VMware enterprise software installed base
- **Key Risk:** Customer concentration (Google ~20% of revenue); AI ASIC commoditization risk long-term; VMware integration execution
- **Rating:** **Buy**

---

### 4. Salesforce (CRM)
- **Thesis:** Down 40% YTD on AI disruption fears, Salesforce is arguably the most mispriced large-cap enterprise software name. Agentforce is showing real enterprise traction (closing deals above $1M ACV), the core CRM business has durable switching costs, and the company is a cash flow machine. The AI threat is overstated for mission-critical CRM workflows.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Forward P/E ~18x; P/S ~5x; EV/EBITDA ~14x; PEG ~0.7x (at current prices)
- **Growth Profile:** Revenue +9–12% YoY; Agentforce driving multi-year reacceleration potential; FCF margins ~33%
- **Competitive Moat:** 23-year customer data relationships; #1 CRM market share; Agentforce AI layer deepens integration
- **Key Risk:** AI agents (Microsoft Copilot, OpenAI) could gradually disintermediate CRM workflows; competitive pressure from HubSpot at the low end
- **Rating:** **Buy**

---

### 5. Applied Materials (AMAT)
- **Thesis:** Semiconductor equipment is a picks-and-shovels play on both AI infrastructure build-out and the global fab expansion (TSMC Arizona, Intel 18A, Samsung Texas). AMAT trades at a ~23% discount to the semiconductor equipment peer group on forward P/E. Equipment spending is a 1–2 year leading indicator of chip production — the AI capex cycle ensures robust demand.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Forward P/E ~26.6x; P/S ~5x; EV/EBITDA ~18x; PEG ~1.1x
- **Growth Profile:** Revenue +15–20% YoY; Gate-all-around (GAA) transistor transition to drive incremental AMAT content per wafer
- **Competitive Moat:** ~20% global semiconductor equipment market share; deep integration in customer process flows (high switching cost); China revenue diversification (with risk)
- **Key Risk:** Export controls limiting China revenue (~25% of total); memory capex can be cut quickly in downturns
- **Rating:** **Accumulate**

---

### 6. ServiceNow (NOW)
- **Thesis:** ServiceNow is positioning as the enterprise AI orchestration layer — the platform that routes and governs AI agent actions across enterprise workflows. Down 36% YTD despite underlying business remaining healthy (30%+ revenue growth). The GPT-5.6 regulatory news validated that enterprises still need platforms like ServiceNow to manage AI governance.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Forward P/E ~35x; P/S ~9x; EV/EBITDA ~28x; PEG ~1.2x
- **Growth Profile:** cRPO (current Remaining Performance Obligations) growing 30%+ YoY; NRR >120%; expanding into AI-native workflows
- **Competitive Moat:** Deep workflow automation installed base across Fortune 500; switching costs extremely high (mission-critical ITSM/HR workflows); Pro Plus AI tier driving ARPU expansion
- **Key Risk:** AI-native competitors (e.g., Glean, Moveworks) attacking from below; premium valuation still requires execution; macro slowdown could delay enterprise IT spending
- **Rating:** **Buy**

## Bottom Line

Today's U.S.–Iran "stand-down" announcement gives the market a tactical relief bounce after a bruising week, but the structural challenges haven't changed: inflation is running at 3.6%, the Fed is leaning hawkish, and AI sector valuations are still digesting a CapEx-versus-returns reckoning. Own the picks-and-shovels, stay selective on the hyped AI "application layer," and size positions conservatively until the geopolitical fog clears.
