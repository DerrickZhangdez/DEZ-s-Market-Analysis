# Daily Market Report — Tuesday, July 21, 2026

## Executive Summary
Markets enter Tuesday cautiously optimistic after Monday's pullback, with Dow and S&P 500 futures higher and chip stocks staging a modest recovery ahead of a pivotal earnings week. The dominant story remains the collision of three forces: a hawkish Fed under new Chair Kevin Warsh weighing a possible July 29 rate hike, easing (but still headline-driving) U.S.-Iran tensions that have pulled oil off its highs, and the start of Big Tech earnings season with Alphabet, Tesla, and Intel all reporting within 72 hours. Today's spotlight sector is Healthcare Tech, Medical Devices & Biotech, where a handful of profitable, reasonably-valued names are being overlooked in favor of AI megacaps.

## Macro & Monetary Environment
The Federal Reserve, now under Chair Kevin Warsh, holds its next policy meeting July 28–29, and the market has been rapidly repricing the odds of a hike: CME FedWatch puts the probability of a 25bp rate increase at 46.5%, up sharply from 34% just days earlier. The Fed funds rate has sat at 3.50%–3.75% for four straight meetings — its highest level in over a decade — and the committee's tone has shifted decisively. Fed Governor Christopher Waller noted the central bank's focus has "completely flipped" from labor-market concerns to inflation containment, after the Fed's own 2026 PCE inflation projection was revised up to 3.6% from 2.7%. This is a meaningfully more hawkish setup than markets were pricing just a month ago, and it is the single biggest swing factor for rate-sensitive equities (small-caps, homebuilders, regional banks) this week.

**What to watch this week:** The July 28–29 FOMC decision and post-meeting presser; a heavy slate of earnings (Alphabet and Tesla after Tuesday's close, Intel Thursday); and any fresh PCE or CPI commentary from Fed officials ahead of the blackout period.

## Geopolitical Factors
Oil has retreated sharply from its highs — Brent near $72/bbl and WTI below $69 — as U.S.-Iran diplomatic talks in Qatar showed signs of progress, pulling a chunk of the geopolitical risk premium out of crude. That said, Monday's session was a reminder of how fragile that calm is: renewed military exchange headlines between the U.S. and Iran briefly drove oil higher and knocked the S&P 500 and Nasdaq lower. Iran remains a ~3.2 million bbl/day producer and key supplier to Asian buyers, so any breakdown in talks could reverse the recent price decline quickly. The IEA's projected global oversupply of 3.7–4.0 million bbl/day is currently capping upside even amid the tension, giving energy markets an unusually two-sided setup heading into the back half of the year.

## Sector Deep Dives

### Today's Spotlight: Healthcare Tech, Medical Devices & Biotech (Real Revenue)
Healthcare has quietly become one of the more attractively valued corners of the market — the S&P 500 healthcare sector's forward P/E of 18.7 compares favorably to the broader index, and analysts on average expect medical device stocks to rise nearly 26% over the next year. Three distinct sub-stories stand out today:

1. **Commercial-stage biotech is being repriced on real cash flow, not just pipeline hope.** Names like Madrigal Pharmaceuticals (Rezdiffra for MASH) and Axsome Therapeutics (Auvelity, Sunosi) are posting 50%+ revenue growth off approved, reimbursed drugs — a very different risk profile than clinical-stage biotech, yet still priced with growth-stock multiples rather than pure blue-chip pharma multiples.
2. **Medtech/devices are trading at a discount to their own history.** Zimmer Biomet's EV/EBITDA of ~10.7x sits roughly 41% below its 10-year median of 18.1x, even as buybacks and EPS guidance have moved higher — a classic "unloved but improving" setup as hospital capital budgets normalize post-pandemic.
3. **Healthcare IT has been punished indiscriminately.** Digital-health platforms like Doximity have sold off (down as much as 40% YTD at the low) on growth-deceleration fears and executive turnover, but the underlying business — 90% gross margins, ~30% net margins — looks nothing like the unprofitable digital-health names that crashed in 2022.

Caution flags in the space: plasma-products name ADMA Biologics cut its FY2026 revenue guidance to $530–560M (from a prior implied $575–600M+) on competitive and channel-inventory pressure, and withdrew long-term guidance — a reminder that not every "cheap" healthcare name is cheap for the right reasons. It was evaluated for today's picks and excluded on that basis.

### Artificial Intelligence
AI capital-expenditure scrutiny is the subtext of this week's entire earnings calendar. Alphabet reports Tuesday after the close, with the Street modeling EPS of $2.88 (+24.7% YoY) and revenue of $117.0B (+21.3% YoY) — but investors are increasingly focused on free cash flow, which fell 47% YoY in Q1 2026 to $10.12B as quarterly capex of $35.7B outpaced operating cash generation. Tesla also reports Tuesday, with the market more focused on robotaxi and Optimus progress than the already-strong 480,126 vehicle deliveries. Intel reports Thursday and, per options markets, carries an implied earnings-day move above its historical 12.4% average — a sign of just how much uncertainty is priced into the AI-capex-driven turnaround narrative.

### Technology (Broad)
Software has been a mixed bag on the analyst-note front today: Morgan Stanley cut Salesforce to Equal Weight (target trimmed to $185 from $287) and downgraded Adobe to Underweight (target slashed to $240 from $365), both citing AI-disruption-to-core-business concerns, while the same desk initiated Microsoft at Overweight with a $600 target. The divergence underscores a market that is increasingly discriminating between AI "enablers" (infrastructure, platforms with clear monetization) and AI "disrupted" (legacy SaaS models facing margin or seat-count pressure).

### Hardware & Semiconductors
Chip stocks were the best-performing pocket of the tape in early Tuesday trading, rebounding after Monday's Iran-driven risk-off move, with Nasdaq-100 futures leading the broader market higher. The setup into Intel's Thursday report is unusually binary given the size of the options-implied move, and the sector remains the most direct read-through for how seriously the market is taking any slowdown in AI infrastructure spending.

### Software & SaaS
Beyond the Salesforce/Adobe downgrades, the SaaS group remains bifurcated between "AI native" vendors gaining share and legacy platform vendors seeing multiple compression as investors question seat-based pricing durability in an agentic-AI world. Enterprise security and infrastructure-adjacent names have generally held up better than horizontal productivity SaaS in recent weeks.

## Stock Picks — Valuation-Based

### 1. MDGL — Madrigal Pharmaceuticals, Inc.
**Thesis:** Madrigal owns the only approved MASH (metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis) therapy, Rezdiffra, and is running one of the strongest specialty-pharma commercial launches in recent memory into a massive, still ~90%-untreated addressable population.
**Valuation Metrics:** Forward P/E ~40.9x–44.7x (trailing not meaningful given recent profitability inflection); market cap ~$11.9B; PEG not broadly applicable given early post-launch ramp; consensus fair-value estimates (~$671) sit meaningfully above the recent trading price.
**Growth Profile:** Q1 2026 Rezdiffra net sales of $311.3M, +127% YoY; FY2025 revenue of $958.4M vs. $180.1M in FY2024 (+432%).
**Competitive Moat:** First-and-only approved MASH drug with patent protection extended to 2045; first-mover advantage in physician/payer education and reimbursement infrastructure that's difficult for follow-on competitors to replicate quickly.
**Key Risk:** Premium growth multiple leaves little room for a launch-trajectory disappointment; competitive MASH therapies in later-stage trials could compress the current near-monopoly.
**Rating:** Buy

### 2. ZBH — Zimmer Biomet Holdings, Inc.
**Thesis:** A core orthopedic-implant franchise trading at a steep discount to its own history, with buybacks and raised EPS guidance signaling management's own view that the stock is undervalued.
**Valuation Metrics:** Trailing P/E ~24.1x, forward P/E ~11.0x; EV/EBITDA ~10.65x vs. a 10-year median of 18.13x (41% discount) and an industry median of ~14.1x.
**Growth Profile:** EPS guidance raised in 2026 alongside an expanded buyback program; procedure volumes normalizing post-pandemic as hospital capital budgets stabilize.
**Competitive Moat:** Scale in knee/hip reconstruction with deep surgeon relationships and instrumentation lock-in; broad distribution network that's expensive for smaller players to replicate.
**Key Risk:** Hospital capital-spending caution and pricing pressure in commoditized implant categories could keep the multiple compressed longer than the "reversion" thesis assumes.
**Rating:** Buy

### 3. AXSM — Axsome Therapeutics, Inc.
**Thesis:** A multi-drug commercial CNS/psychiatry portfolio (Auvelity, Sunosi, Symbravo) with a newly expanded label — Auvelity's April 2026 FDA approval for Alzheimer's-related agitation — that meaningfully broadens the total addressable market beyond depression.
**Valuation Metrics:** Forward P/E ~20.6x on a blended basis; P/S ~18.1x, above the pharma industry average (~6.7x) but roughly in line with a peer-adjusted fair multiple (~18.2x).
**Growth Profile:** Q1 2026 total net product revenue of $191.2M, +57% YoY; Auvelity net sales +59% YoY to $153.2M.
**Competitive Moat:** First-in-class oral mechanism (dextromethorphan/bupropion) with limited direct generic competition and an expanding label that extends exclusivity-protected revenue runway.
**Key Risk:** Elevated P/S leaves the stock reliant on continued 50%+ growth; any deceleration in Auvelity uptake or agitation-label adoption would compress the multiple quickly.
**Rating:** Accumulate

### 4. DOCS — Doximity, Inc.
**Thesis:** The dominant physician-networking and digital-workflow platform for U.S. clinicians has sold off up to 40% in 2026 on growth-deceleration fears and a CFO transition, leaving a highly profitable, capital-light business trading at a discount to healthcare-services peers.
**Valuation Metrics:** P/E ~16.9x vs. Global Healthcare Services industry average of ~28.6x; forward P/E closer to ~12.5x; ~90% gross margins, ~30% net margins.
**Growth Profile:** FY2026 revenue of $644.9M, +13% YoY; free cash flow of $255.3M, underscoring profitability even as top-line growth moderates from prior years.
**Competitive Moat:** Network effects from being the default professional platform for a majority of U.S. physicians; high switching costs for pharma marketing/telehealth partners embedded in the platform.
**Key Risk:** Growth deceleration narrative could persist if pharma marketing budgets (a key revenue driver) soften further, and leadership turnover (CFO resignation) adds near-term execution uncertainty.
**Rating:** Accumulate

## Risk Factors
1. **Hawkish Fed pivot** — A July 29 rate hike (now priced at ~46.5% probability, up from 34%) would be the first hike in years and could hit rate-sensitive small-caps and homebuilders hardest, ironically the exact cohort that has led 2026 performance.
2. **AI capex scrutiny in earnings season** — Alphabet's falling free cash flow (-47% YoY) despite rising capex is exactly the kind of data point that could trigger a broader re-rating of AI infrastructure spenders if Tesla and Intel show similar patterns this week.
3. **Iran/oil volatility** — Oil's retreat to $69–72/bbl is predicated on continued diplomatic progress; a breakdown in U.S.-Iran talks could quickly reverse the move and reintroduce inflation risk right as the Fed is already leaning hawkish.
4. **Inflation re-acceleration** — The Fed's own upward revision to its 2026 PCE forecast (2.7% to 3.6%) suggests policymakers see more persistent price pressure than the market had assumed, raising the risk of a "higher for longer" regime repricing across risk assets.
5. **Concentration risk into mega-cap earnings** — With Alphabet, Tesla, and Intel all reporting within 72 hours and implied volatility elevated (Intel's implied move above its 12.4% historical average), index-level volatility risk is unusually front-loaded this week.

## Bottom Line
This is a week where the macro (Fed hawkishness), the geopolitical (Iran/oil), and the micro (Big Tech AI-capex earnings) are all colliding within a 72-hour window, and small-caps' 2026 outperformance make them the most exposed to a hawkish surprise. Investors with a fundamentals-first approach should look past the crowded AI-megacap trade toward pockets like healthcare tech and devices, where real revenue growth and reasonable multiples still coexist — while keeping enough dry powder to react to what is likely to be a volatile back half of the week.
