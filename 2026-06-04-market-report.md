# Daily Market Report — Wednesday, June 4, 2026

## Executive Summary

U.S. equity markets retreated broadly on Wednesday as fresh U.S.-Iran military strikes pushed crude oil above $96 per barrel, triggering a classic risk-off rotation just one session after Broadcom's blowout Q2 earnings had lifted sentiment. The S&P 500 fell 0.74%, the Dow shed 1.21%, and the Nasdaq dropped 0.89%, with technology, financials, and consumer discretionary leading the decline. AI-related names bore the brunt of selling — Nvidia, Dell, and Microsoft each lost 3%+ while Oracle tumbled more than 5% — reversing a powerful multi-week rally that had pushed the S&P to record territory as recently as last week. Despite today's selloff, the structural bull case for AI infrastructure spending remains intact: Big Tech's combined 2026 capex commitment now exceeds $600 billion, and Broadcom just guided to $16 billion in AI revenue for its current quarter — up 143% year-over-year.

## Macro & Monetary Environment

### Federal Reserve Stance
The Fed held the federal funds rate steady at **3.50%–3.75%** at its April 29 FOMC meeting — the third consecutive hold — with the vote an unusually contentious **8-4**, the most dissents since October 1992. That split reflects genuine internal debate about whether policy is sufficiently restrictive as inflation remains stubborn.

### Inflation
Core PCE printed at **2.8%** in the most recent reading, above the 2.7% consensus and up from 2.6% prior — a worrying re-acceleration. Headline inflation is now getting an additional push from oil, which has spiked on Middle East supply disruptions. The Fed's 2% target remains elusive more than five years into its inflation-fighting campaign.

### Rate Outlook
CME FedWatch prices in a **~98% probability of no change** at the June meeting. However, December rate hike odds have climbed to nearly **30%** — a meaningful shift that markets have not fully priced. Any further energy-driven CPI surprise could pull the first rate hike forward and reprice risk assets sharply.

### Key Data to Watch This Week
- Friday **Non-Farm Payrolls (May)** — the labor market's resilience will determine whether the Fed can remain patient
- Weekly initial jobless claims (Thursday)
- Any Fed speaker commentary on the Iran energy shock and its inflation implications

## Geopolitical Factors

### U.S.–Iran Military Escalation — The Dominant Risk
Today's market selloff was catalyzed by reports of new U.S. and Iranian military strikes, reigniting fears of a wider conflict in the Persian Gulf. The market is now discounting a scenario some analysts are describing as the **most significant energy crisis since the 1970s**.

**Strait of Hormuz:** Roughly 20% of global oil supply transits this chokepoint. Disruptions to shipping lanes and attacks on regional oil and gas infrastructure have already contributed to a sustained risk premium in crude markets. WTI crude settled at **$96.02/barrel (+2.41%)** on Wednesday, briefly topping $100 in intraday trading during peak uncertainty.

**Portfolio implications:**
- Long energy / defense is the near-term tactical hedge
- Rate-sensitive equities face a double headwind: higher oil → higher inflation → higher-for-longer rates
- Emerging market equities with oil import dependencies (India, South Korea, Taiwan) face FX and current account pressure
- TSMC's geographic exposure to Taiwan adds a secondary geopolitical risk layer that investors must monitor

## Sector Deep Dives

### Artificial Intelligence

Today was a digestion day for AI names after a powerful rally. The sector pulled back sharply:

- **Nvidia (NVDA):** -3%+ on Wednesday, reacting partly to Broadcom's Q2 report, which beat on AI revenue ($10.8B, +143% YoY) but did not raise its *full-year* AI semiconductor revenue guidance. Markets interpreted the lack of a forward raise as a read-through signal for the broader AI capex cycle. Despite today's retreat, the NVDA thesis remains intact — CEO Jensen Huang recently called Marvell Technology the next "trillion-dollar company," underscoring the breadth of the AI silicon buildout.

- **Microsoft (MSFT):** -3%, weighed by both macro risk-off and concerns about near-term cloud spend growth.

- **Meta (META):** Relatively resilient versus peers. Meta is committing $115–135B in capital expenditures in 2026, and is working directly with Broadcom on custom ASIC chip designs — a pivot that could reduce long-term dependence on Nvidia.

- **Alphabet (GOOGL):** Announced plans to raise $80B via new equity issuance to fund AI infrastructure — an unusual step that signals both the scale of AI ambition and potential near-term dilution risk. The company plans to allocate $180–190B in total capex in 2026.

- **Anthropic:** Filed for an IPO at a $965B valuation — a landmark event that, if successful, would be one of the largest U.S. tech listings in years. **SpaceX** is separately slated to debut on Nasdaq on **June 12, 2026**.

**Big Picture:** Big Tech's aggregate 2026 AI infrastructure spend now exceeds **$600 billion** across the five major hyperscalers (Amazon, Alphabet, Microsoft, Meta, Oracle). This is not a narrative — it is booked capex, and it flows directly to chip designers, foundries, memory makers, and power infrastructure companies.

### Software & SaaS

The software sector is navigating its post-"SaaSpocalypse" recovery. In early February 2026, Anthropic's Claude Cowork platform sparked fears that AI agents would render per-seat SaaS licensing obsolete, wiping **$285B** from software valuations. The panic proved premature:

- **Snowflake (SNOW):** Surged **+36%** on blowout Q1 2026 earnings (reported May 28):
  - Revenue growth: +33% YoY
  - Added 616 net new customers (+38% YoY)
  - 779 customers now spending >$1M annually
  - Raised FY2027 revenue guidance to **$5.84B**
  - However, profit-taking hit the stock on June 3 (-5.8%)

- **ServiceNow (NOW):** Down **-18.4% YTD**, trading **42.4% below** its 52-week high of $208.94 set in July 2025. The selloff is largely SaaSpocalypse collateral damage — the Now Platform's deep integration into enterprise ITSM workflows makes it far less vulnerable to AI agent displacement than feared. Fell an additional 6.1% on June 3.

- **Oracle (ORCL):** Down **>5%** today, despite reporting strong numbers recently. Oracle's cloud infrastructure business (OCI) is a direct beneficiary of AI hyperscaler spend diversification.

- **Salesforce (CRM):** Bucked the recent software rally with lackluster guidance that sent shares modestly lower. Near-term headwinds from enterprise budget scrutiny remain.

- **The IGV software ETF** rose **21% in May** — its best monthly performance since October 2001 — and crossed back into positive YTD territory by June 2, sitting approximately 11% below its all-time high.

## Stock Picks — Valuation-Based

### 1. Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing (TSM)
- **Thesis:** TSMC is the irreplaceable backbone of the global AI buildout — no major AI chip runs without it. The 3nm price hike of up to 15% in H2 2026 demonstrates extraordinary pricing power at full capacity utilization. At ~24x forward earnings, it trades at a meaningful discount to Nvidia and Broadcom despite facing no competitive threat in leading-edge logic.
- **Valuation Metrics:**
  - Forward P/E: ~24x
  - Trailing P/E: ~35–39x
  - Market Cap: >$2.28 trillion
  - Note: Intrinsic value estimates vary widely; consensus views the stock as fairly-to-richly valued, though growth offsets
- **Growth Profile:** Revenue growing at a strong double-digit rate driven by AI chip orders; 3nm capacity ramping aggressively
- **Competitive Moat:** Only company capable of manufacturing leading-edge AI chips at volume — NVDA, AMD, Apple, and Google all depend on TSMC
- **Key Risk:** Taiwan-China geopolitical risk and potential supply chain disruption; concentration in a single geography
- **Rating: Buy**

At 42% below its 52-week high and -18.4% YTD, the market is pricing in a structural impairment that the fundamentals don't support. - **Competitive Moat:** High switching costs via Now Platform embedded in mission-critical enterprise IT; multi-year contracts; large installed base
- **Key Risk:** Prolonged AI-agent disruption narrative could keep multiple depressed; Salesforce/Microsoft competitive pressure in certain workflow categories
- **Rating: Accumulate** (building a position at current levels; target full position below $115)

### 4. Micron Technology (MU)
- **Thesis:** Micron is the cheapest quality stock in the AI semiconductor ecosystem. At a forward P/E of ~12x — versus the integrated systems industry median of ~17x — it is being valued as a commodity memory cyclical even as its HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) business transforms it into a structural AI infrastructure beneficiary. Every AI accelerator requires vast amounts of HBM, and Micron is one of only three suppliers globally (alongside SK Hynix and Samsung).
- **Valuation Metrics:**
  - Forward P/E: ~12x (vs. industry median ~17x)
  - Deeply discounted vs. Nvidia, AMD, Broadcom on any earnings or sales multiple
  - P/S: Below 3x forward revenue — attractive for a high-growth AI memory play
- **Growth Profile:** HBM revenue growing exponentially as AI model size scales; DRAM/NAND cycle recovery on top of structural AI demand
- **Competitive Moat:** One of only three HBM suppliers; strong U.S./Japan manufacturing footprint well-positioned for CHIPS Act subsidies
- **Key Risk:** Memory cycle downturn, excess DRAM/NAND supply outside HBM, Samsung/SK Hynix competition
- **Rating: Buy**

### 5. Qualcomm (QCOM)
- **Thesis:** At ~19.5x forward earnings, Qualcomm is the most attractively valued large-cap tech company in the mobile-to-AI transition. The company is executing a successful diversification from smartphone modems into on-device AI chips (Snapdragon X Elite for PCs), automotive, and IoT — reducing its Apple dependence while expanding into faster-growing end markets. The AI PC cycle is still in its early innings.
- **Valuation Metrics:**
  - Forward P/E: ~19.5x
  - Strong free cash flow generation; consistent dividend growth
  - PEG ratio: Attractive given growth reacceleration from AI PC and automotive segments
- **Growth Profile:** AI PC chip wins accelerating; automotive design win pipeline multi-year; licensing business provides high-margin recurring revenue floor
- **Competitive Moat:** Qualcomm's Snapdragon AI architecture leads in on-device inference efficiency; 35+ years of wireless IP licensing creates an uncuttable royalty stream
- **Key Risk:** Apple in-sourcing of baseband chips could reduce Qualcomm's smartphone exposure; ARM ecosystem competition from Apple, MediaTek, and Ampere
- **Rating: Accumulate**

## Risk Factors

Ranked by near-term severity:

1. **U.S.–Iran Military Escalation (CRITICAL):** New strikes today pushed WTI above $96. A direct hit on major oil export infrastructure or Strait of Hormuz closure could spike oil to $120–150+, generating a 1970s-style stagflation shock that would force the Fed to hike rates into a slowing economy — the worst possible macro outcome for equities.

2. **Inflation Re-acceleration + Fed Policy Error (HIGH):** Core PCE at 2.8% with energy prices now rising adds to existing stickiness. The 8-4 FOMC split signals internal pressure to hike; if the June CPI print surprises to the upside, markets will rapidly price in a summer rate hike, triggering a repricing of high-multiple tech names.

3. **AI Capex Cycle Sustainability (MEDIUM-HIGH):** Big Tech is committing $600B+ in AI capex for 2026, but ROI visibility remains limited. Any signal from Alphabet, Microsoft, or Amazon that AI monetization is lagging infrastructure spending could trigger a sharp de-rating in AI-linked hardware names. Broadcom's decision not to raise full-year AI revenue guidance despite a monster quarter is an early yellow flag.

4. **China-Taiwan Geopolitical Risk (MEDIUM):** With the U.S. military engaged in the Middle East, Taiwan Strait risk is arguably at a cyclical high. A TSMC disruption scenario — however low-probability — would be catastrophic for the entire global technology supply chain. Investors in TSM, NVDA, AMD, and ASML should size positions accordingly.

5. **Software Multiple Compression (MEDIUM):** The SaaSpocalypse narrative — AI agents displacing per-seat SaaS — is not dead, just dormant. Salesforce's lackluster guidance and continued ServiceNow weakness remind investors that enterprise software faces a genuine structural question about AI's impact on licensing models. If Anthropic's Claude Cowork platform gains traction, a second wave of multiple compression could materialize.

---

## Bottom Line

Today's selloff is geopolitical noise sitting on top of a structurally intact AI bull market — Broadcom's $10.8B AI revenue quarter and the $600B+ in committed hyperscaler capex are real, not rhetorical. The immediate playbook is to let AI names digest after the May melt-up, use any further weakness driven by oil/Iran headlines to add to high-conviction semiconductor and AI infrastructure positions (AVGO, TSMC, MU), and selectively accumulate the most oversold software names where the SaaSpocalypse fear has created genuinely attractive entry points (NOW). The principal tail risk is an oil shock severe enough to force the Fed's hand on rate hikes — that scenario warrants a meaningful defensive hedge in energy equities and a reduction in duration-sensitive tech multiples.
