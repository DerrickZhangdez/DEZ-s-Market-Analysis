# Daily Market Report — Friday, July 24, 2026

## Executive Summary
Wall Street is trying to stabilize Friday after a sharp Thursday sell-off in which the Nasdaq shed more than 2% and the S&P 500 lost over 1%, driven by AI-spending anxiety following Alphabet's earnings and a punishing reaction to Tesla's results. Oil remains the other dominant storyline: crude has carried a geopolitical risk premium for weeks on Strait of Hormuz shipping disruptions, though prices eased modestly Friday morning as diplomatic talks progressed. Investors are also bracing for next week's FOMC meeting (July 28–29), the first under Chair Kevin Warsh with real hike odds on the table, against a backdrop of inflation still running near 3.6–3.7%, well above target. Volatility jumped sharply on Thursday (VIX +12%), and today's focus is on whether megacap AI capex concerns broaden into a wider growth-stock repricing or stay contained to a handful of names.

## Macro & Monetary Environment
The federal funds rate has been held at 3.50%–3.75% for four consecutive meetings, but the policy conversation has turned more hawkish. Under new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh, inflation projections for 2026 have been revised up to roughly 3.6%, with the latest print near 3.7% — well above the Fed's 2% target. The FOMC meets July 28–29, and markets are currently pricing a 25–30% probability of a rate hike at that meeting, with two hikes broadly expected before year-end, potentially starting in September or October. This is a meaningful shift from the cutting-cycle narrative that dominated markets earlier in the year, and it is a key reason bond-proxy and long-duration growth names have been under pressure. Watch this week: the FOMC statement and Warsh press conference, any fresh PCE/CPI commentary, and continued corporate guidance on tariff pass-through costs.

## Geopolitical Factors
Middle East tensions remain the dominant geopolitical driver of energy markets. Renewed US-Iran hostility around the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly one-fifth of global oil and LNG flows transit — has pushed shipping traffic through Omani waters down to single digits per day versus a pre-conflict average of roughly 100, sharply raising insurance costs and reducing vessels' willingness to transit the Gulf. Brent crude fell about 2% Friday morning to just under $99/bbl as diplomatic talks between the US and Iran in Qatar offered a glimmer of de-escalation, but the broader risk premium remains firmly embedded given the US's limited inventory cushion heading into peak summer demand. Separately, new Trump-administration tariffs took effect this week, adding another layer of cross-border cost uncertainty for multinational earnings.

## Sector Deep Dives

### Today's Spotlight: Enterprise Software & SaaS
After a stretch of AI-infrastructure-heavy coverage, today's deep dive rotates to enterprise software — a group that got hit disproportionately hard in 2025 on fears that AI would cannibalize traditional per-seat SaaS models, even as fundamentals for many names stayed intact. That sentiment/fundamentals gap is exactly where this section is hunting for value.

Large-cap bellwethers illustrate the setup: Salesforce trades near 14x forward earnings and about 10x next-twelve-months EBITDA despite guiding revenue from $41.5B toward $63B over the medium term, while ServiceNow grew 2025 revenue 21% to $13.3B and guides to another ~20–21% in 2026 — yet both have seen multiple compression as investors demand proof that AI agents expand rather than shrink software spend.

Below the megacaps, the more interesting opportunities are in profitable-or-near-profitable mid-caps with real AI-driven product attach, rather than pure AI-narrative stocks. JFrog's DevOps/software-supply-chain platform grew revenue 26% y/y in Q1 with net retention at 120% and cloud now over half of revenue — the market has started to price in an "AI winner" premium, and valuation (11.3x EV/FY26 revenue) reflects that. Procore's vertical construction-management SaaS continues posting mid-teens growth with 80% gross margins even as its stock sits down nearly 24% YTD, an unusual disconnect for a company that just raised guidance. monday.com and Five9 both show the pattern investors want post-2025: decelerating-but-durable top-line growth (18–19% and low-to-double-digit respectively) paired with visibly improving margins and, in Five9's case, AI-driven subscription revenue up 68% y/y. At the value end of the spectrum, legacy infrastructure-software consolidators like Progress Software screen cheap on both P/E and P/S versus the software group while running 40%+ EBIT margins — the tradeoff is minimal organic growth, funded instead by disciplined M&A.

Net takeaway: enterprise software valuations have reset enough that several names are now priced more like mature industrials than AI disruption targets, which is precisely the gap this week's picks below are designed to exploit.

### Artificial Intelligence
AI-spending anxiety was the single biggest driver of Thursday's sell-off. Alphabet's earnings triggered a roughly 7% drop in the stock as investors focused on the scale of incremental AI infrastructure capex rather than the underlying cloud and search results, reviving the "is AI capex outrunning AI revenue" debate that has periodically rattled megacap tech all year. Intel and SAP both reported Thursday after the close, adding to a busy 48 hours of read-throughs on enterprise AI demand and chip capacity. Coverage-reference names NVIDIA, Microsoft, Meta, TSMC, AMD, Broadcom, ASML and AMAT remain the primary AI capex/supply-chain bellwethers investors are using to triangulate whether the spending cycle is peaking or merely pausing.

### Technology (Broad)
Tesla was the other major earnings casualty, falling roughly 14% on its report — a reminder that growth-multiple stocks are being priced with far less patience than a quarter ago. Cloud and internet names broadly tracked the Nasdaq's weakness Thursday before futures stabilized Friday morning. Analyst activity was active mid-week: CoreWeave was upgraded to Buy from Hold at Truist Financial (price target trimmed to $126), DigitalOcean was raised to Buy from Hold at Stifel (target raised to $160), and AT&T was upgraded to Outperform at Wolfe Research. On the downgrade side, Vornado Realty was cut to Underweight at Morgan Stanley and Norwegian Cruise Line was downgraded to Hold at Truist — both idiosyncratic rather than broad-tech reads, but indicative of analysts trimming exposure into a choppier tape.

### Hardware & Semiconductors
Chip-stock momentum, which had been carrying the broader tape, faded earlier this week even before Thursday's AI-capex-driven selloff, with oil's rise adding a cost-side headwind for energy-intensive fabs and data centers. Intel's after-hours print Thursday is a key read on foundry and PC-cycle demand heading into the weekend. Equipment names (ASML, AMAT, LRCX-type suppliers) remain the cleanest proxy for whether hyperscaler and foundry capex plans announced earlier this year are holding up under the current rate and inflation backdrop.

Beyond the spotlight names, JPMorgan's upgrade to Buy at Deutsche Bank (target to $375) is a reminder that financial-data and fintech infrastructure platforms are also being repriced this week, and Digital Ocean's upgrade suggests cloud-infrastructure sentiment is troughing even as pure-play AI infrastructure names wobble.

## Stock Picks — Valuation-Based

### 1. FROG — JFrog Ltd.
- **Thesis:** DevOps/software-supply-chain platform benefiting from being positioned as an "AI winner" — cloud migration (now >50% of revenue) and 120% net retention show real enterprise pull, not just narrative.
- **Valuation Metrics:** P/E: not meaningful (GAAP unprofitable); P/S: ~15.7x TTM (market cap $9.63B vs. TTM revenue $563M); EV/EBITDA: not meaningful (pre-EBITDA-profitability at scale); PEG: N/A given current losses, though narrowing.
- **Growth Profile:** Revenue +26% y/y in Q1 2026; forecast net loss per share narrowing to $0.52 from $0.62.
- **Competitive Moat:** Entrenched developer-workflow tooling (artifact management) with high switching costs once embedded in CI/CD pipelines.
- **Key Risk:** Valuation is already stretched (11.3x EV/FY26 revenue, ~48x EV/FCF) — any growth deceleration would compress the multiple quickly.
- **Rating:** Accumulate

### 2. PCOR — Procore Technologies, Inc.
- **Thesis:** Vertical construction-management SaaS leader trading near 24% below its YTD high despite raising 2026 guidance — a rare growth-at-a-reasonable-price setup in vertical software.
- **Valuation Metrics:** P/E: not meaningful (still GAAP unprofitable); P/S: 5.6x; EV/EBITDA: not meaningful on a GAAP basis (269x reflects minimal current GAAP EBITDA, not a clean multiple); PEG: not meaningful given early profitability inflection.
- **Growth Profile:** Q1 2026 revenue +16% y/y to $359M with 80% gross margins; FY2026 guidance of $1.50B implies ~13.6% growth at the high end.
- **Competitive Moat:** Deep integration into construction workflows and a large, sticky customer base of contractors/owners with high data lock-in.
- **Key Risk:** Growth has been decelerating from historical rates; construction-cycle sensitivity to rates and tariffs on materials could pressure end-market demand.
- **Rating:** Buy

### 3. MNDY — monday.com Ltd.
- **Thesis:** Work-management "Work OS" platform expanding into CRM and dev-tooling modules, showing the growth-plus-margin combination the market is rewarding post-2025 SaaS reset.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Forward P/E: ~31.7x; Trailing P/E: ~36x; P/S: ~5.2x forward (below the ~6.2x software industry average); EV/EBITDA: not separately reported but consistent with mid-single-digit revenue multiple and expanding margins; PEG: roughly 1.2–1.7x using forward P/E against ~19–26% growth.
- **Growth Profile:** FY2026 revenue guided to $1.45–1.46B (+18–19% y/y); adjusted operating margin guided to 11–12%; adjusted FCF margin 19–20%.
- **Competitive Moat:** Broad, sticky multi-module platform with strong SMB-to-enterprise land-and-expand motion.
- **Key Risk:** Increasing competition from both point solutions and megacap collaboration suites (Microsoft, Google) bundling similar functionality for free/low cost.
- **Rating:** Buy

### 4. FIVN — Five9, Inc.
- **Thesis:** Contact-center-as-a-service platform where AI subscription revenue is growing 68% y/y and now represents 13% of subscription revenue — a genuine AI-monetization story rather than an AI-disruption target.
- **Valuation Metrics:** P/E: ~33x; P/S: ~2.5x approximate (based on ~$1.26B FY2026 revenue guidance and current market cap in the low-$3B range); EV/EBITDA: mid-teens approximate given $74M quarterly adjusted EBITDA at a 24% margin; PEG: roughly 1.0–1.5x depending on forward growth assumption.
- **Growth Profile:** Q1 CY2026 revenue +9.2% y/y to $305.3M; FY2026 revenue guidance raised to $1.26B midpoint; adjusted gross margin 64% (+200bps y/y).
- **Competitive Moat:** Deep telephony/CX integrations and a growing AI-agent layer that raises switching costs versus legacy on-prem contact center systems.
- **Key Risk:** Total revenue growth has slowed to single digits — the AI narrative needs to keep accelerating to justify the multiple as core CCaaS growth matures.
- **Rating:** Accumulate

### 5. PRGS — Progress Software Corporation
- **Thesis:** A genuine value name in enterprise infrastructure software — cheap on both earnings and sales multiples versus peers, with elite margins funded by disciplined, accretive M&A rather than organic hypergrowth.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Trailing P/E: ~17–19x; Forward P/E: high-single-digits per some estimates (~7.7x), reflecting embedded acquisition synergies; P/S: 1.66x vs. ~3.78x industry average; EV/EBITDA: attractive given EBITDA margin above 50%; PEG: elevated on organic growth alone (~2%+ ARR growth) but more reasonable including M&A-driven earnings growth.
- **Growth Profile:** FY2026 revenue guidance raised to ~$990M–$1.0B (roughly 1–2.5% reported growth); Q2 2026 revenue +7% y/y to $253M; EBIT margin 42.3%, EBITDA margin >50%.
- **Competitive Moat:** Diversified portfolio of entrenched, high-switching-cost infrastructure and application-development tools acquired at disciplined prices; strong free cash flow funds continued consolidation.
- **Key Risk:** Organic ARR growth is nearly flat — the thesis depends on continued smart capital allocation via M&A, and a bad acquisition or rising rates raising deal costs would undercut the model.
- **Rating:** Buy

## Risk Factors
1. **Hawkish Fed pivot into a fragile tape** — a 25–30% probability of a July 29 hike, against inflation still near 3.6–3.7%, could extend the multiple compression seen in Thursday's sell-off, especially in unprofitable growth names.
2. **AI capex/monetization gap widening** — Alphabet's and Tesla's earnings reactions show markets are no longer giving megacap AI spenders a free pass; a similar reaction to upcoming reports (Intel, further hyperscalers) could broaden the drawdown.
3. **Strait of Hormuz escalation** — despite this week's modest de-escalation, tanker traffic remains near-frozen versus pre-conflict norms; any breakdown in US-Iran talks could send crude sharply higher and reignite inflation/growth-shock fears simultaneously.
4. **New tariffs taking effect** — the tariffs that began this week add fresh, still-unquantified cost pressure to multinational earnings and could complicate the Fed's inflation read.
5. **Volatility regime shift** — VIX jumping over 12% in a single session signals thinning liquidity and higher sensitivity to incremental bad news heading into a heavy week (FOMC, continued earnings).

## Bottom Line
Markets are digesting a genuine growth-versus-value repricing in tech, not just a one-day wobble, with the Fed's more hawkish tone and Middle East oil risk compounding the pressure. Enterprise software's 2025 sentiment washout has left several mid-cap names — JFrog, Procore, monday.com, Five9 and Progress Software — trading on more reasonable fundamentals than the AI-capex names that drove Thursday's selloff, making this a better hunting ground for valuation-based buyers than chasing the megacap AI trade into next week's FOMC decision.
