## Macro & Monetary Environment

### Federal Reserve — Warsh's First Meeting

Kevin Warsh's debut as Fed Chairman concluded Wednesday with a unanimous vote to **hold rates at 3.5%–3.75%**, but the tone was unmistakably hawkish:

- **Dot plot shift:** The median funds rate projection for year-end moved to **3.8%** — above the current target range — signaling a hike is firmly on the table.
- **9 of 18 FOMC members** project at least one rate hike by year-end; 6 see two hikes.
- Rate **cuts pushed entirely to 2027–2028**, a dramatic revision from earlier expectations.
- **PCE inflation** was revised sharply upward to **3.6%** for 2026 (from 2.7%); core PCE in May came in at **2.9%**.
- Headline inflation has climbed to **~4.2%**, the highest in more than three years, partly attributed to supply disruptions from the Iran war.

### Key Takeaways for Investors

The Fed has effectively abandoned its easing cycle. The bond market's reaction — a surge in yields Wednesday — is the true transmission mechanism. Rate-sensitive sectors (utilities, REITs, long-duration growth) face renewed headwinds. The shift to a potential hiking bias also puts pressure on stocks with stretched multiples where the discounted cash flow thesis depended on falling rates.

**This week's economic data to watch:** Weekly jobless claims (Thursday), PCE Price Index revision (Friday), University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment (Friday).

## Geopolitical Factors

### US-Iran Interim Peace Deal (Primary Catalyst Today)

The most market-moving development of the past 24 hours is the **signing of a US-Iran interim peace agreement**, announced overnight. This is the primary driver behind Thursday's futures rally. Key market implications:

- **Oil risk premium deflating:** Brent crude was already averaging ~$60/bbl per J.P. Morgan's 2026 forecast, but the Strait of Hormuz blockage risk had embedded a significant geopolitical premium into prices. An interim deal meaningfully reduces near-term supply disruption risk.
- **Energy stocks mixed:** While lower oil prices are deflationary (broadly positive), integrated energy producers face margin compression.
- **Risk appetite returning:** The Iran conflict had been described as triggering "the most significant energy crisis since the 1970s." Any de-escalation is broadly positive for global equity risk premiums.

### Remaining Geopolitical Risks

- **Strait of Hormuz:** The interim deal does not eliminate risk. Approximately 20% of global oil flows through the strait. A deal reversal or new provocation would immediately re-inject risk premiums.
- **Middle East broader instability:** Regional military positioning and proxy dynamics remain in place even with a bilateral US-Iran agreement.
- **Trade policy:** Tariff uncertainty continues to weigh on global supply chains and corporate earnings guidance visibility.

## Sector Deep Dives

### Artificial Intelligence

The AI investment supercycle continues at a scale that is difficult to overstate. The four primary hyperscalers — **Google (Alphabet), Amazon, Microsoft, and Meta** — collectively plan to deploy approximately **$725 billion in capital expenditures in 2026**, up a staggering **77%** from the already-record $410 billion in 2025. This spending is the backbone of AI infrastructure demand.

**NVIDIA (NVDA):** The undisputed AI accelerator king with an estimated **80–90% market share** in high-end GPU accelerators. Revenue growth has exceeded 60% in recent periods, supported by the Blackwell platform rollout. However, the stock pulled back more than **$700 billion in market cap** from its brief $5 trillion valuation peak earlier this year. The question for investors is whether the pullback represents a buying opportunity or the early stages of a valuation reset.

**Microsoft (MSFT):** Tracking toward **$190 billion in CapEx** for calendar 2026. The company is widely seen as significantly undervalued relative to peers given its deep Azure + Copilot integration across enterprise workflows. The Copilot monetization story is still in early innings.

**Alphabet (GOOGL):** Approaching a **$4 trillion market cap** as Wall Street begins assigning real financial value to TPU commercialization. Morgan Stanley estimates Alphabet could ship **500,000–1 million TPUs per year by 2027**, opening a new high-margin hardware revenue stream alongside Google Cloud and advertising.

**Meta Platforms (META):** Guiding to **$115–$135 billion in CapEx** for 2026. AI-driven content recommendation and ad efficiency improvements are already visible in margins, providing internal funding for the massive infrastructure buildout.

### Technology (Broad)

The broader tech sector is in a bifurcated state: **mega-cap AI infrastructure plays** are consuming capital but generating massive returns, while **mid-cap software** is recovering from a brutal "SaaSpocalypse" narrative that briefly hammered valuations by 40–60%.

The key narrative shift: AI is increasingly seen as **augmenting** enterprise software rather than replacing it outright. Mission-critical data, customer relationships, and workflow platforms are the layers that AI agents need to function inside large enterprises — and that insight is rehabilitating the SaaS sector.

### Hardware & Semiconductors

The semiconductor sector experienced a notable shakeout earlier in June following **Broadcom's (AVGO) Q3 AI chip guidance** of $16 billion — below the $17.2 billion analyst consensus. That miss sent Broadcom down **14%** on June 4 and dragged AMD, Intel, and Qualcomm lower in sympathy. However, the structural thesis remains intact:

- **TSMC (TSM):** Q1 2026 revenue grew **35.1% YoY**; January–May 2026 revenue up **30% YoY** vs. 2025. Full-year 2025 revenue was $122.9 billion (+31.6%). Deloitte's 2026 Semiconductor Industry Outlook projects global chip sales reaching a **historic $975 billion** in 2026. TSMC trades at approximately **24x forward earnings** — compelling for a company growing revenue at 30%+.
- **Broadcom (AVGO):** Q2 AI semiconductor revenue hit **$10.8 billion (+143% YoY)**, with Q3 AI revenue guided to grow **200%+ YoY to $16 billion**. The company's roadmap targets **>$100 billion in AI chip revenue by 2027** through custom ASIC design for OpenAI and Meta (2nm SoCs offering 3x performance-per-watt vs. GPUs). Remaining performance obligations under contracts: **$164.6 billion**.
- **AMD:** Slipped ~3% in the June 5 semiconductor selloff. Positioned as an alternative GPU supplier to NVIDIA but facing a wide moat gap.
- **Intel:** Continuing its foundry transformation; down ~1% in the June 5 selloff. Recovery timeline remains uncertain.

### Software & SaaS

The "SaaSpocalypse" thesis — that AI agents would kill traditional SaaS revenue — is being dismantled in real time.

**Snowflake (SNOW):** Surged **~37%** on earnings (best day since its 2020 IPO) after demonstrating that the AI data cloud thesis is intact and growing. Results helped alleviate broader fears that AI would displace SaaS incumbents.

**ServiceNow (NOW):** Rallied **6%+** in the Snowflake-led software rally, though gave back gains Wednesday on Fed-rate concerns. ServiceNow's executive Amit Zavery has articulated a compelling centralized AI operations platform thesis — customers need systems that monitor AI usage, costs, and operational activity alongside traditional software. ServiceNow owns exactly that workflow layer.

**Oracle (ORCL):** Up **6%+** in the May/early-June software rally, then retreated **~2.5%** in the most recent session alongside broader market weakness. Oracle's cloud infrastructure (OCI) business is gaining traction as an alternative hyperscaler; its database dominance remains an AI-era data moat.

**Salesforce (CRM):** Bucked the Snowflake rally with shares **down ~4.1%** after lackluster guidance. Salesforce faces the strongest AI agent displacement narrative of the major SaaS platforms given its CRM focus, though Agentforce represents its strategic response.

## Risk Factors

Ranked by near-term severity:

1. **Fed Tightening Cycle Resumption (HIGH):** With 9 of 18 FOMC members favoring a rate hike and PCE inflation at 3.6%, a hike by September or November is a live scenario. This would re-price long-duration assets, compress growth multiples, and potentially re-invert the yield curve. Biggest impact on high-P/E tech, unprofitable growth, and REITs.

2. **Iran Deal Collapse / Strait of Hormuz (HIGH):** Thursday's rally is built on an *interim* peace deal. A breakdown in negotiations, a military incident in the Persian Gulf, or resumption of Strait of Hormuz threats would immediately re-price energy and global risk assets.

3. **AI CapEx Sustainability / ROI Questions (MEDIUM-HIGH):** $725 billion in 2026 hyperscaler CapEx is predicated on AI monetization ramping to justify the investment. If enterprise AI adoption disappoints, or if competitive dynamics drive down AI pricing faster than expected, CapEx could be cut abruptly — with severe consequences for NVIDIA, TSMC, and infrastructure plays.

4. **Semiconductor Demand Deceleration (MEDIUM):** Broadcom's Q3 guidance miss on June 4 — even though it represented massive absolute growth — sent the sector down double-digits. Investor tolerance for any growth deceleration in high-expectation chip stocks is extremely low. A miss from TSMC or NVIDIA would have amplified market-wide consequences.

5. **US Dollar Strength / Emerging Market Contagion (MEDIUM):** The hawkish Fed pivot is strengthening the dollar, creating stress in EM sovereign debt and currency markets. A dollar-driven EM crisis could spill over into global risk appetite and reduce demand for US exports, particularly technology hardware.

## Bottom Line

The market faces a genuine tension between a re-accelerating AI buildout (the most powerful secular growth driver since mobile internet) and a Federal Reserve that is now leaning toward tightening rather than easing — the worst macro backdrop for long-duration growth equities. Today's Iran deal relief rally is a valid tactical tailwind but does not resolve the structural rate uncertainty. **For patient investors, the AI infrastructure supply chain (TSMC, Broadcom) and undervalued AI-integrated software (Adobe, Microsoft) offer the best combination of fundamental backing and realistic upside.** Avoid reaching into speculative AI names at peak multiples — the valuation discipline Warsh's Fed is imposing will punish hype and reward earnings quality.
