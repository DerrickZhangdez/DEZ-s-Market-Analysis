## Macro & Monetary Environment

**Federal Reserve — On Hold, One Eye on the Door**

The Federal Open Market Committee left the federal funds rate unchanged at **3.50%–3.75%** at its June 16–17 meeting, the latest decision under newly installed Fed Chair **Kevin Warsh**, who assumed the chair position in May 2026. The statement confirmed that inflation "remains elevated relative to the Committee's 2 percent goal," citing supply-shock contributions in energy — an unmistakable reference to the Strait of Hormuz closure disrupting global oil logistics since late February.

The FOMC's June projections revised PCE inflation sharply upward to **3.6% for 2026** (from 2.7%) and **3.3% for 2027** (from 2.7%), reflecting the second-order pass-through of energy costs into food, freight, and manufactured goods. Critically, **nine of 18 FOMC members** now project at least one rate *hike* before year-end 2026, and six see two increases — effectively sweeping away market hopes for 2026 easing that lingered just a quarter ago.

**The next FOMC decision is July 28–29.** Policy expectations lean toward another hold, but Chair Warsh has signaled a willingness to move if June PCE data (released July 25) comes in above forecast. Markets are pricing approximately a 22% probability of a July hike — non-trivial but not the base case.

## Geopolitical Factors

**Strait of Hormuz — The World's Most Critical Chokepoint, Week 19**

The de facto closure of the Strait of Hormuz has now exceeded three months since military action dramatically curtailed shipping traffic beginning February 28, 2026. Approximately **20% of global seaborne oil and 25% of global LNG** ordinarily transits the strait, making this the most consequential energy supply shock since the 1970s Arab oil embargo.

Key developments and market implications:

- **Brent crude averaged $107/barrel in May**, a $10/barrel *decline* from April's peak as ceasefire reports and U.S.-Iran diplomatic back-channels generated cautious optimism. Prices have remained volatile in June–July as talks have progressed fitfully.
- **OECD oil inventories** are forecast to fall to just under 2.3 billion barrels by December 2026 — the lowest since 2003 and roughly 500 million barrels below the prior five-year average — underscoring the real physical scarcity building in the system.
- **Europe faces acute vulnerability:** Natural gas storage stood at approximately 38% of seasonal capacity as of late May, well below historical norms, driven by suspended Qatari LNG shipments.
- **Asia's front-line exposure:** Pakistan, Japan, and the Philippines each sourced over 90% of crude oil imports from Persian Gulf sources, creating genuine supply-security risk if the current stalemate extends into Q3.
- **U.S. food and fertilizer prices** face upward pressure: roughly one-third of global fertilizer shipments transit the strait, with agricultural input cost increases beginning to surface in commodity markets.

The market continues to price a *baseline scenario* in which U.S.-Iran negotiations produce a framework agreement allowing partial Hormuz reopening by late Q3 2026. Any breakdown in that negotiation process would be a significant negative catalyst for global growth and re-ignite inflationary pressure just as the Fed is monitoring for a hike opportunity.

**Secondary Geopolitical Watch:** U.S.-China trade tensions remain a persistent background risk, with tariff regimes still in place across a broad range of goods. Export controls on advanced semiconductor equipment and chip technology to China continue to weigh on names with >15% China revenue exposure.

## Sector Deep Dives

### Today's Spotlight: AI Infrastructure & Data Centers (Sector Index 2 of 6)

**The Trillion-Dollar Plumbing Problem — And Why It's Far From Solved**

The dominant AI investment narrative of 2024–2025 centered on chips: GPUs, HBM memory, and semiconductor equipment. That narrative was correct, but it captured only the most visible layer of a far deeper infrastructure buildout. By mid-2026, the capital allocation story has migrated *down the stack* — toward the physical systems that actually keep AI workloads running: power delivery, thermal management, optical interconnects, and edge networking.

The numbers are staggering. Hyperscalers — Microsoft, Meta, Alphabet, and Amazon — have guided combined capex above **$320 billion for 2026**, flowing into a supplier ecosystem that most institutional investors still underweight. Global data center power demand is now forecast to grow **+220% from 2023 levels**, reaching 1,350 terawatt-hours annually by 2030 — a figure revised significantly upward from prior estimates of +175% as AI server shipment volumes have exceeded initial projections.

**Why the AI Infrastructure Trade Remains Early:**
The crucial insight for investors is that AI infrastructure companies do not require AI model performance to continue improving — they simply require *continued deployment* of AI workloads at the current pace. Inference demand (running models in production, not just training them) is growing faster than anticipated and is proving more power-intensive than initial architecture estimates suggested. The "picks and shovels" framing is correct, but only a fraction of investors have moved from identifying the trade to constructing concentrated, durable positions.

**Key Sub-Sectors Within AI Infrastructure:**

**1. Thermal Management & Liquid Cooling**
The transition from air-cooled racks (typically handling 10–15kW per rack) to liquid-cooled high-density AI racks (handling 60–100kW per rack and beyond) is creating explosive, durable demand for specialized cooling equipment. Liquid cooling is not merely a preference at these power densities — it is physically mandatory. The thermal management addressable market is estimated at $20–40 billion cumulatively through 2030, with data center thermal applications growing at 50–70%+ annually through at least fiscal 2028.

Modine Manufacturing's landmark **$4 billion long-term capacity agreement** (2027–2029) for Airedale data center cooling products — with an upfront $165 million cash payment — is the clearest recent signal of hyperscaler urgency to lock in cooling capacity years in advance.

**2. Power Infrastructure & Electrical Construction**
Data center campuses increasingly require dedicated power generation and high-voltage transmission line construction — work that is entirely different from the standard commercial construction lifecycle. Electrical infrastructure backlogs for companies like Quanta Services have reached unprecedented levels: Quanta reported a **$48.5 billion total backlog** in Q1 2026 (up from $44 billion as recently as 2025), reflecting multi-year visibility that rivals even the strongest SaaS ARR growth stories. The company plans to invest $500–700 million to double its power transformer manufacturing capacity as grid constraints become the binding constraint on new AI campus timelines.

**3. Optical Interconnects & Photonics**
As AI clusters scale from thousands to hundreds of thousands of GPUs interconnected at 3.2 terabit/second speeds, copper-based interconnects physically cannot carry the required bandwidth over relevant distances. Optical transceivers, lasers, and optical circuit switches are the mandatory upgrade path. NVIDIA's March 2026 announcement of a **$2 billion strategic investment in Lumentum** — alongside multibillion-dollar purchase commitments for advanced laser components — is the strongest validation signal the photonics industry has received in its history.

**4. Edge Networking, AI Security, & CDN**
As AI inference workloads push computation toward the edge (for latency reasons), the network fabric routing those workloads becomes a critical chokepoint. Cloudflare's emerging AI Gateway product — which routes, caches, rate-limits, and observes LLM API traffic — is turning the company into essential infrastructure for any organization running AI in production, not just a CDN or firewall vendor.

**5. Renewable Power Generation**
Data center operators have made aggressive commitments to carbon-neutral or carbon-negative operations, creating structural demand for long-term renewable power purchase agreements with domestic solar and wind generators. First Solar — the dominant U.S.-based solar panel manufacturer with the most advanced thin-film cadmium telluride technology — is the primary beneficiary of both clean energy mandates and the Inflation Reduction Act's domestic content incentives.

## Risk Factors

Ranked by probability-weighted severity to market returns over the next 1–4 weeks:

**1. Strait of Hormuz — Ceasefire Breakdown (CRITICAL)**
The dominant tail risk for global markets. If U.S.-Iran negotiations collapse and the ceasefire ends, Brent crude could spike to $130–150+/barrel, reigniting inflation at a pace that forces the Fed to move aggressively in Q3 2026. **2. Fed Over-Tightening / July Hike Surprise (HIGH)**
If the June CPI print (July 14) or PCE (July 25) comes in materially above forecast, Chair Warsh — who has been more hawkish than his predecessor — may move at the July 28–29 meeting. The current investment thesis assumes sustained $300B+ combined hyperscaler capex; any reduction in that assumption is structurally negative for the AI infrastructure names in today's picks.

**4. China Export Control Escalation (MEDIUM)**
A new tranche of semiconductor export restrictions — particularly targeting AI chip exports and advanced chip-making equipment — would impair several large-cap semi names and introduce supply chain uncertainty for the AI infrastructure buildout. Watch for Commerce Department announcements or retaliatory measures from China.
