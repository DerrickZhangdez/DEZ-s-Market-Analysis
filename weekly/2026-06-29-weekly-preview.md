## The Week in One Paragraph

Wall Street enters the last trading week of Q2 with a split personality: macro tailwinds are accumulating at a pace not seen in years — a U.S.-Iran resolution is bringing the Strait of Hormuz crisis to a close, oil has shed more than $40 from its 2026 peak, and JPMorgan just raised its S&P 500 target to 7,800 on a "Blue Sky" scenario — yet the Nasdaq suffered its worst week since spring, tumbling 4.6% as AI spending doubts, an OpenAI IPO delay, and chip sector rotation weighed heavily on sentiment. The dominant theme this week is the **June nonfarm payrolls report** (Thursday, July 2 — moved one day earlier due to the July 4 holiday), with consensus at +172,000 jobs, which will either validate the higher-for-longer rate view or cement the case for rate relief later in 2026. Fed Chair Kevin Warsh speaks Tuesday morning, and an otherwise light earnings calendar (Nike, Constellation Brands, General Mills) will not distract from macro. The biggest near-term risk: a strong payrolls print re-ignites rate-hike fears that were already moving higher last week.

## Positioning & Strategy

**Stay Selective, Not Defensive.** The macro backdrop is improving — Hormuz resolution, oil down ~40% from peak, inflation expectations likely to moderate — but the growth narrative in tech has taken a credibility hit that will take more than one week to repair. This is an environment where **sector rotation** (not risk-off) is the dominant trade. Cyclicals (industrials, materials, energy services companies pivoting to domestic), value names, and consumer discretionary beneficiaries of lower gas prices deserve a higher weight in portfolios today than they did in Q1.

**The Payrolls Trade.** Thursday's jobs report is a binary event. Weak print (<150K): buy the belly of the curve (5Y Treasury), add to rate-sensitive equities (utilities, REITs, homebuilders), reduce dollar exposure. Strong print (>200K): stay defensive, consider adding VIX calls as insurance, avoid long-duration plays. The base case (+172K) supports a continued hold path through July 29 and doesn't dramatically move markets — set and monitor.

**The Hormuz Dividend.** The single biggest underappreciated catalyst this week is confirmation of Strait of Hormuz reopening. If formal announcements progress, Brent could fall further toward $75-80, directly reducing headline CPI risk, compressing the Fed's hawkish bias, and potentially catalyzing a broad multi-sector rally that catches over-hedged portfolios offside. Airlines, ground transportation, chemicals, and consumer discretionary are the key beneficiaries of a $20–30/bbl oil decline from current levels.

---

## Bottom Line

**The most important call this week: Buy the payrolls dip.** If Thursday's jobs number comes in at or below consensus (+172K), the market has its clearest green light of the quarter — a Fed on hold, oil declining, Q2 earnings season about to reveal solid results, and a new all-time high on the S&P 500 within striking distance. The Nasdaq selloff last week was painful but not fundamental — AI infrastructure spending is slowing its rate of acceleration, not reversing. The real earnings season starts July 7. Use any Thursday post-payrolls weakness as an accumulation opportunity in quality names. JPMorgan's 7,800 target for the S&P 500 by year-end is credible; the path there runs through this week's data without a hawkish shock.
