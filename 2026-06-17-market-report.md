## Macro & Monetary Environment

### The Warsh Era Begins

Kevin Warsh opened his first FOMC meeting as Federal Reserve Chair on June 16–17, 2026 — the most closely watched monetary policy event of the year. The Fed is holding the federal funds rate at **3.50%–3.75%** for the fourth consecutive meeting, a stance supported by virtually every major Wall Street economist. More important than the rate decision itself is the updated Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) and fresh dot plot, which will give markets the first official signal of whether Warsh is inclined to pivot more hawkish than his predecessor.

### Inflation Still Running Hot

Persistent energy-driven inflation has been the central macro story of 2026. Both consumer and producer price data have run hotter than expected, and with Brent crude at multi-year highs, the PCE path remains uncomfortably elevated above the Fed's 2% target. The labor market remains resilient with unemployment near **4.4%**, removing any urgency for emergency rate relief.

A Reuters poll conducted in early June 2026 showed nearly **70% of economists** expect the fed funds rate to remain at 3.50%–3.75% for the remainder of the year. The base case is that Warsh will use today's press conference to establish a clear, data-dependent framework — likely signaling fewer than two cuts for 2026 in the revised dot plot.

### What to Watch This Week

- **Today (June 17):** Warsh FOMC press conference ~2:30 PM ET; updated dot plot and SEP
- **Thursday:** Initial jobless claims, housing starts
- **Friday:** Preliminary June University of Michigan consumer sentiment

## Geopolitical Factors

### US–Iran: Deal Done, But Strait of Hormuz Still Closed

The single largest macro wildcard in markets right now is the US-Iran conflict and its aftermath. After an exchange of military strikes, a peace framework was announced — sending the Dow to fresh record highs — but the critical bottleneck is the **Strait of Hormuz**, through which roughly **20% of global oil shipments** pass. Officials have warned that physically reopening shipping lanes could take months, not days, keeping the oil market in a structural risk premium.

**Brent crude** briefly surpassed **$110/barrel** and touched **$117 in peak sessions**, levels not seen since the 2022 energy crisis. Even if the ceasefire holds, the market appears to be pricing in a prolonged normalization timeline.

### Key Energy/Geopolitical Risks

- **Strait of Hormuz shipping disruption** — Any re-escalation would immediately spike Brent toward $130+
- **Sanctioned Iranian supply off-market** — Sustained removal of Iranian barrels is structurally bullish for oil
- **Defense sector tailwinds** — Elevated Middle East tensions are supporting aerospace and defense spending globally
- **Tanker and shipping rates** — Rerouting around the Cape of Good Hope is inflationary for supply chains globally

## Sector Deep Dives

### Artificial Intelligence

The AI spending supercycle remains structurally intact, but the market is more discerning about valuation in mid-2026 than it was 18 months ago.

**NVIDIA (NVDA)** reported Q1 FY2027 earnings on May 20, 2026, with staggering results: **$81.6 billion in revenue** (+85% YoY), beating consensus estimates of $78.8B. Adjusted EPS of $1.87 topped the $1.76 estimate. CEO Jensen Huang declared Blackwell GPU sales are "off the charts," with cloud GPUs sold out. Data center revenue reached approximately $62.3 billion. Despite the beat, NVDA shares experienced a post-earnings "sell the news" reaction as the stock had already priced in perfection at 30x forward earnings. Analyst consensus remains **Strong Buy** with 62 covering analysts, citing Blackwell ramp, inference workload expansion, robotics, and edge AI as growth vectors beyond the current cycle.

**Alphabet/Google (GOOGL)** is approaching a **$4 trillion market capitalization** as Wall Street finally assigns concrete financial value to its Tensor Processing Unit (TPU) business. Google Cloud's AI attach rate is rising, and internal TPU deployments are now being treated as a strategic moat in competition with AWS and Azure.

**Meta Platforms (META)** is executing a significant chip diversification strategy, contracting to rent Google Cloud TPUs starting in 2026 and planning to bring Google TPUs into its own data centers by 2027. This reduces dependency on NVDA while maintaining AI infrastructure scale. Meta's Llama open-source model ecosystem continues to attract enterprise developers.

**Microsoft (MSFT)** gives investors clean exposure to Azure AI growth and OpenAI-linked enterprise demand through Copilot, which continues to see strong commercial uptake.

### Technology (Broad)

### Hardware & Semiconductors

The hardware/semiconductor sector is navigating a volatile moment. The **June 5 semi sell-off** — triggered by Broadcom's Q3 AI chip sales guidance of $16B falling short of the $17.2B analyst estimate — reminded investors that even the strongest demand cycles can produce expectation gaps.

**TSMC (TSM):** The indispensable node in the AI hardware supply chain. Every major AI chip — NVDA Blackwell, AMD MI400, custom ASICs at Google, Apple, and Broadcom — is manufactured in TSMC's fabs. Q1 2026 revenue was approximately **$35.6 billion (+35% YoY)**, surpassing consensus. TSMC raised full-year revenue growth guidance above 30%. At approximately **24x forward earnings**, TSMC is materially cheaper than NVDA (~30x) and Broadcom, despite delivering comparable or superior earnings growth. This is one of the most compelling risk/reward setups in the sector.

**AMD (AMD):** Shares fell 10.86% to $466.38 in the June 5 sell-off. However, analyst consensus projects **39% upside** over the next 12 months, driven by the MI400 AI GPU ramp and continued market share gains in data center CPUs vs. Intel. AMD's AI roadmap is credible and the sell-off may represent an attractive entry.

**Broadcom (AVGO):** Q2 2026 revenue of $22.19B beat consensus ($22.13B), and non-GAAP EPS of $2.44 topped the $2.39 estimate. However, Q3 AI chip sales guidance disappointed. Broadcom's AI semiconductor revenue is still projected to exceed **$30 billion in FY2026**, and the company's dual role in custom AI accelerators (XPUs) and AI networking infrastructure is strategically unassailable.

**Intel (INTC):** Continued to struggle in the June 5 sell-off. Intel's foundry turnaround remains a multi-year story; not recommended at this stage without clearer evidence of execution progress.

**ASML / AMAT:** Both are longer-cycle plays. Equipment makers benefit from TSMC's CapEx ramp but face a 6–12 month lag. Watch for ASML booking data as a leading indicator of fab expansion into 2027.

### Software & SaaS

Enterprise software is staging one of the most dramatic recoveries in recent memory. After entering 2026 with a brutal narrative — "AI agents will kill traditional software" — that sent many SaaS names down 40–60% from their 52-week highs, the sector has violently reversed. The **iShares Expanded Tech-Software ETF (IGV)** is up **35% from its April 2026 low**, and the recovery has broadened from initial AI-native winners to the entire category.

**Snowflake (SNOW):** The standout performer of the recovery. Shares surged approximately **50% in four trading days** after announcing a **$6 billion cloud and chip deal with Amazon** while raising guidance. Snowflake's AI data platform thesis — that enterprises need a neutral, cloud-agnostic data layer for AI/ML workloads — is being validated at scale.

**ServiceNow (NOW):** Surged over **20%** as AI agent integrations within the Now Platform are being adopted rapidly across enterprise IT, HR, and legal workflows. ServiceNow's moat in workflow automation makes it a natural platform for AI agent deployment.

**Oracle (ORCL):** Up approximately **16%** in the recent recovery. Oracle's OCI (Oracle Cloud Infrastructure) is increasingly positioned as the cloud of choice for regulated industries and AI training workloads. Partnerships with NVIDIA and a growing AI database workload are structural tailwinds.

**Salesforce (CRM):** The relative laggard. Shares declined slightly after lackluster guidance at its most recent earnings report, as the market questioned the pace of Agentforce monetization. The thesis is intact but execution needs to improve. Salesforce is on watchlist, not a buy today.

## Stock Picks — Valuation-Based

### 1. Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing (TSM)
- **Thesis:** The single most essential company in the global AI supply chain, yet trades at a significant discount to the pure-play AI names it enables. Every dollar spent on AI chips flows through TSMC's fabs first.
- **Valuation Metrics:** ~24x forward P/E (vs. ~30x for NVDA, ~28x for AVGO); P/S ~10x; strong free cash flow generation
- **Growth Profile:** Q1 2026 revenue +35% YoY to ~$35.6B; raised full-year guidance above 30% growth; EPS growth tracking above 30% on operating leverage
- **Competitive Moat:** Technological lead in advanced process nodes (2nm, A16) is effectively a 3–5 year moat; no credible competitor at scale
- **Key Risk:** Taiwan geopolitical tension — any escalation with China would create severe near-term dislocation, though the global dependency on TSMC would also create enormous diplomatic incentive to protect it
- **Rating:** ⭐ **Strong Buy**

### 2. Micron Technology (MU)
- **Thesis:** Micron is the most undervalued company in AI infrastructure. Its High Bandwidth Memory (HBM3E) is a required component in every NVDA H100/H200/Blackwell GPU, yet MU trades at a fraction of the valuation of its downstream customers.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Single-digit forward P/E; one of the cheapest valuation setups in the entire tech sector given the growth profile; P/S substantially below DRAM cycle peers
- **Growth Profile:** 50%+ forecast revenue growth into fiscal 2026 driven by HBM3E ramp and AI-driven memory demand; EPS expected to swing from loss to massive profitability
- **Competitive Moat:** Only three companies (Micron, Samsung, SK Hynix) can produce HBM at scale; US-based production gives Micron strategic geopolitical advantage in a tariff/export-restricted world
- **Key Risk:** Memory cycle volatility — DRAM oversupply can return quickly if hyperscaler CapEx moderates; storage (NAND) remains cyclically weaker
- **Rating:** ⭐ **Strong Buy**

### 3. AMD (Advanced Micro Devices) (AMD)
- **Thesis:** The June 5 sector sell-off created an attractive entry point in the clearest second-source AI GPU play in the market. The MI400 ramp is real, and hyperscalers are motivated to maintain competition with NVDA to keep pricing rational.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Shares at ~$466 post-sell-off represent ~39% discount to analyst consensus price targets; forward P/E in the mid-20s, PEG ratio improving meaningfully as AI GPU revenue ramps
- **Growth Profile:** AI GPU revenue growing from a small base but inflecting sharply; data center CPU market share gains vs. Intel continuing; full-year 2026 EPS estimates have not been cut despite the stock drop
- **Competitive Moat:** x86 CPU duopoly with Intel; only credible GPU alternative to NVIDIA for hyperscaler workload diversification; CDNA4 architecture on track
- **Key Risk:** NVDA's execution advantage widens further; any delay in MI400 ramp or hyperscaler customer defection back to NVDA would reprice the stock
- **Rating:** **Buy**

### 5. ServiceNow (NOW)
- **Thesis:** AI agents are not killing enterprise software — they're being deployed through it. ServiceNow is the workflow layer through which AI agents will operate in every Fortune 500 company, making it a direct AI monetization vehicle without GPU capex risk.
- **Valuation Metrics:** Post-recovery valuation still below prior highs; forward P/E in high 30s appears reasonable for a business with 20%+ revenue growth and 85%+ gross margins; RPO (remaining performance obligations) growth signals durable demand
- **Growth Profile:** Revenue growing 20%+ YoY; AI agent deployments are driving seat expansion and incremental ACV per enterprise; management has raised FY2026 guidance
- **Competitive Moat:** Once a company standardizes on the Now Platform across IT, HR, and legal, switching costs are extraordinarily high; 7,700+ enterprise customers represent a durable renewal base

## Risk Factors

Ranked by severity and near-term market impact:

1. **🔴 CRITICAL — Strait of Hormuz Re-Escalation:** The Iran ceasefire is fragile. Any military re-escalation that closes the Strait would immediately spike Brent crude toward $130–$140/barrel, triggering a risk-off rotation, equity multiple compression, and likely emergency Fed considerations. This is the tail risk of the moment.

2. **🔴 HIGH — Warsh Hawkish Pivot Signal:** If the updated dot plot today shows the FOMC projecting fewer cuts or a later timeline than expected (e.g., no cuts until 2027), expect a sharp repricing of rate-sensitive assets — tech growth stocks, small caps (Russell 2000), and real estate would all face headwinds.

3. **🟡 MEDIUM — AI Capex Disappointment:** The $300B+ hyperscaler capex commitment for 2025–2026 is priced into semiconductor and AI infrastructure valuations. Any signal from Microsoft, Google, Amazon, or Meta that projects are being paused or delayed would trigger a cascading sell-off from NVDA through TSMC to equipment makers.

4. **🟡 MEDIUM — Software AI Valuation Reset:** The enterprise software recovery rally of 35% from April lows is pricing in a rapid monetization of AI agent capabilities. If Q2 earnings (beginning in July) show delayed customer adoption or lower-than-expected AI upsell revenue, the rally could give back 15–20% quickly.

5. **🟢 LOWER — Taiwan Geopolitical Risk:** Elevated US-China trade tensions and Taiwan Strait military activity remain a persistent background risk. While a near-term military incident is not the base case, the TSMC concentration of global chip manufacturing means any escalation would be a systemic market event, not just a single-stock risk.

## Bottom Line

Today's market is a microcosm of the broader 2026 investment environment: powerful structural tailwinds from AI and enterprise technology modernization, set against an inflation-constrained Fed, elevated energy prices from the Middle East, and a market that has priced in a great deal of good news. The most actionable near-term catalyst is **Warsh's press conference this afternoon** — investors should position defensively ahead of it and look for buying opportunities in quality AI infrastructure names (TSMC, Micron) if the dot plot disappoints. The software recovery is real and has fundamental backing; ServiceNow and Snowflake's AI-agent monetization stories are worth accumulating on any broader tech pullback.
