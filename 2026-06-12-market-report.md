## Macro & Monetary Environment

**Fed Stance:** The Federal Open Market Committee held the federal funds rate at **3.50%–3.75%** at its April 2026 meeting — the third consecutive pause — and the minutes signal a strongly data-dependent posture. Market odds on Polymarket and Kalshi imply near-zero probability of a June cut, with the first cut now being priced for Q4 2026 at the earliest.

Core CPI remains above 3%, well above the Fed's 2% target. April CPI had already printed at 3.8% YoY (+0.6% MoM), raising the policy bar for any near-term easing.

- **PPI data mid-week** — a second consecutive hot PPI print would further cement the "higher for longer" narrative.

## Geopolitical Factors

**US–Iran / Strait of Hormuz (Severity: CRITICAL)**
The Strait of Hormuz has been de facto closed to normal shipping traffic since February 28, 2026, when military action began. The closure has been in effect for over 100 days, representing the longest sustained disruption to this chokepoint in modern history. Brent crude averaged $107/barrel in May — elevated but notably $10/bbl below April's average — as the market began pricing in the possibility of a deal. President Trump's Thursday decision to cancel threatened military strikes against Iran, coupled with signals that both sides are near agreement on a ceasefire extension, drove a sharp pullback in oil. OECD inventories are projected to fall to a critical 50-day supply cover by end-2026 if the disruption persists — the tightest buffer since January 2003.

**Russia–India–China Oil Triangle**
Nearly 70% of Russian crude is now under restriction. The Trump administration has tied lower US tariffs on India to a reduction in Russian oil imports; India is partially complying, redirecting flows toward China. This fracturing of the global oil trade network is creating persistent pricing distortions and logistics complexity across Asian refiners.

**US–China Trade / Semiconductor Export Controls**
NVIDIA is actively marketing its Vera CPU to Chinese clients — targeting delivery as early as August — in a bid to recover market share lost to export controls on its advanced Blackwell and Hopper-class GPUs. The Biden-era controls have been maintained and tightened under the current administration. Any escalation in chip export restrictions represents a meaningful tail risk for the semiconductor supply chain.

**SpaceX / xAI Governance Risk**
SpaceX completed its acquisition of Elon Musk's xAI this year prior to its IPO, creating a combined entity at the intersection of aerospace, AI infrastructure, and satellite connectivity. The concentration of transformative technology under a single founder introduces governance and conflict-of-interest risk that institutional investors are still pricing in.

## Sector Deep Dives

### Artificial Intelligence

The AI investment supercycle shows no signs of deceleration. Amazon, Microsoft, Google (Alphabet), and Meta collectively committed to approximately **$710 billion in AI infrastructure investment** by 2026, primarily targeting data center expansion and GPU compute capacity. This level of capital commitment is historically unprecedented and has fundamentally altered the supply/demand picture for semiconductors, power infrastructure, and cloud services.

**NVIDIA (NVDA):** Remains the dominant platform for AI training and inference. The company is pushing its Vera CPU architecture toward China markets while Blackwell demand stays robust in the US and EMEA. Forward P/E of ~22–25x against expected EPS growth of 25–35% gives a PEG of ~0.9x — surprisingly reasonable for the most consequential infrastructure company of the decade.

**Microsoft (MSFT):** Azure remains OpenAI's primary cloud partner while also hosting Anthropic's Claude and proprietary Copilot models across Microsoft 365, GitHub, and Windows. The enterprise Copilot adoption curve is accelerating, particularly in regulated industries where Azure's compliance posture is a differentiator.

**Alphabet (GOOGL):** Gemini 3 is powering AI Overviews for billions of Google Search users and the Gemini consumer app. Custom TPU chips give Google a meaningful cost advantage vs. NVIDIA-dependent competitors, though monetization of AI-enhanced search remains a work in progress.

**Meta (META):** Despite facing increasing regulatory scrutiny in the EU and UK, Meta continues aggressive AI infrastructure buildout and open-source model releases (Llama series). Analysts see revenue upside as AI improves ad targeting precision; the risk is regulatory-driven platform restrictions on data usage.

### Hardware & Semiconductors

**TSMC (TSM):** The irreplaceable center of gravity for advanced logic. Q1 2026 revenue of **$35.6 billion (+35% YoY)** blew past consensus, and management raised full-year revenue growth guidance to **above 30%**. With 2nm nodes entering volume production in 2026 and capacity for advanced packaging (CoWoS) fully booked through year-end, TSMC is operationally firing on all cylinders. It trades at ~24x forward earnings — a meaningful discount to NVIDIA (~32x) and Broadcom (~41x) despite comparable or superior growth visibility.

**Broadcom (AVGO):** Reported fiscal Q2 2026 revenue of **$22.19 billion**, beating estimates on both revenue and non-GAAP EPS. However, the stock was pressured after management guided Q3 AI chip sales to **$16 billion vs. the $17.2 billion Street estimate**, and notably did not raise its full-year 2026 AI semiconductor sales forecast. This guidance miss triggered the June 5 semiconductor sell-off that dragged AMD, Intel, and Micron sharply lower. The sector has since partially recovered on Iran relief, but Broadcom's muted guide warrants attention.

**AMD (AMD) & Intel (INTC):** Both sustained heavy losses in the June 5 sector sell-off following Broadcom's cautious AI chip guide, compounded by a memory chip demand warning and deteriorating smartphone demand forecasts. Both have substantial YTD gains that leave them vulnerable to incremental negative data points. AMD's MI300X AI accelerator remains a credible NVIDIA alternative; Intel's Gaudi 3 has shown limited commercial traction.

**ASML (ASML) & Applied Materials (AMAT):** Semiconductor equipment makers continue to benefit from TSMC and Samsung CapEx expansion for 2nm and 1.4nm nodes. ASML's EUV and High-NA EUV monopoly is structurally unassailable through the decade; AMAT benefits from etch, deposition, and CMP intensity increases at each process node shrink.

### Software & SaaS

The software sector just wrapped its **best month since 2001 in May 2026**, decisively burying the "SaaSpocalypse" narrative that had driven several enterprise software names to 52-week lows. The recovery thesis: businesses are not ripping out their Oracle ERPs and Salesforce CRMs to replace them with AI agents — they are buying AI to sit *on top of* existing platforms, making the incumbents more valuable, not obsolete.

**Snowflake (SNOW):** The week's standout performer. Shares surged **+36% in a single session** — the stock's best day ever — after the company beat earnings estimates on AI-driven momentum, guided full-year product revenue to $5.84 billion (above consensus), and announced a **5-year, $6 billion commitment to purchase compute capacity from Amazon Web Services**. The AWS deal is dual-purpose: it deepens the cloud partnership while providing scale guarantees that underpin Snowflake's AI data platform ambitions. After the gap-up, valuation is no longer deep-value but the growth trajectory justifies a premium.

**ServiceNow (NOW):** Gained over 20% in recent sessions and is now among the software sector's top performers YTD. ServiceNow executive Amit Zavery noted at the Jefferies conference that enterprises are actively seeking "centralized systems that can monitor AI usage and costs" — a use case where NOW's platform is uniquely positioned. The AI-as-workflow-orchestrator narrative is NOW's to win.

**Oracle (ORCL):** Dropped **~10% to $182.25** following its Q4 FY2026 earnings report, which disappointed on cloud growth metrics despite headline revenue being adequate. Oracle's cloud infrastructure business (OCI) remains a distant third behind AWS and Azure, and the company's AI positioning is more dependent on licensing its database for AI workloads than on winning new cloud-native workloads.

**Salesforce (CRM):** Down **35% YTD** — one of the worst-performing large-cap tech names of 2026. The AI disruption overhang has been severe; investors fear that Microsoft Dynamics + Copilot and AI-native CRM startups will erode Salesforce's dominant position. However, the contrarian case is building: the installed base is enormous, the Agentforce AI platform is gaining enterprise traction, and the valuation is now approaching historical trough multiples. A catalyst — strong Q2 print or CEO Marc Benioff's more aggressive AI roadmap communication — could reverse the trend sharply.

## Stock Picks — Valuation-Based

*All picks are premised on a 12–18 month holding horizon with current prices as of June 11, 2026 close.*

### 1. Micron Technology (MU) — Strong Buy

**Thesis:** Micron is the most statistically undervalued large-cap semiconductor in the market. The company is the primary beneficiary of the HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) supercycle driven by AI accelerators — every NVIDIA H100/H200/B200 and AMD MI300X requires massive HBM3/HBM3e content from Micron (alongside SK Hynix). The market is applying trough-cycle multiples to what is increasingly a structurally elevated demand environment.

**Valuation Metrics:**
- Forward P/E: ~12.2x (vs. sector avg ~25x)
- P/S: ~4.5x
- PEG Ratio: **0.2x** — extreme undervaluation relative to projected growth
- EV/EBITDA: ~8x (trough multiple on cyclical)

**Growth Profile:**
- Q2 FY2026 EPS guidance: ~$8.42 per quarter
- Full-year FY2026 consensus EPS: ~$33
- Revenue growth: +50%+ YoY driven by AI memory demand

**Competitive Moat:** Micron and SK Hynix have a duopoly on HBM production at scale for AI; Samsung trails in yield rates. DRAM and NAND pricing power is recovering.

**Key Risk:** Memory is inherently cyclical; a datacenter capex pause or HBM oversupply event would compress margins rapidly.

**Rating: Strong Buy**

### 2. TSMC (TSM) — Strong Buy

**Thesis:** TSMC is the irreplaceable foundry for every leading-edge AI chip. With 2nm entering volume production in 2026, and CoWoS advanced packaging capacity fully committed to NVIDIA and Apple, TSMC's pricing power and utilization rates are structurally elevated. It trades at a significant discount to its customers despite arguably superior earnings quality.

**Valuation Metrics:**
- Forward P/E: ~24x (vs. NVIDIA ~32x, Broadcom ~41x)
- PEG Ratio: ~0.9x
- EV/EBITDA: ~18x

**Growth Profile:**
- Q1 2026 Revenue: $35.6B (+35% YoY)
- FY2026 revenue growth guidance: **>30%**
- Free cash flow generation strong enough to fund Arizona fabs AND sustain dividend growth

**Competitive Moat:** Only company in the world with volume 2nm and 3nm production. Switching costs for a hyperscaler are measured in years and billions of dollars.

**Key Risk:** Taiwan geopolitical risk (China invasion scenario) remains the existential risk; US export controls limiting China revenues (~15% of sales) are a margin headwind.

**Rating: Strong Buy**

---

### 3. Marvell Technology (MRVL) — Buy

**Thesis:** Marvell is the leading custom ASIC supplier for hyperscaler AI accelerators — a less-visible but high-margin role that makes it one of the most compelling AI infrastructure picks outside NVIDIA. Data center revenue now exceeds 70% of total sales and is growing at 50%+ YoY, driven by custom silicon programs at Amazon (Trainium/Inferentia) and Google (TPU).

**Valuation Metrics:**
- PEG Ratio: ~0.6x — significant discount to growth
- Forward P/E: ~28x
- P/S: ~10x

**Growth Profile:**
- Data center revenue: 70%+ of total, growing ~50% YoY
- Total revenue expected to re-accelerate as hyperscaler ramp cadence increases

**Competitive Moat:** Deep co-design relationships with AWS and Google that take 2–3 years to replicate; strong IP in DSPs, PAM4 optical interconnects, and SerDes.

**Key Risk:** Heavy customer concentration (top 2 customers ~40% of revenue); a hyperscaler capex pause or in-house chip success would be a direct headwind.

**Rating: Buy**

### 4. Cisco Systems (CSCO) — Accumulate

**Thesis:** Cisco is the overlooked AI infrastructure beneficiary. Every GPU cluster deployed in a hyperscaler or enterprise data center requires a spine of high-bandwidth Ethernet switching, routers, and optical transceivers — Cisco's core products. The AI networking upgrade cycle is just beginning, and Cisco trades at a material discount to its sector while paying a ~3.2% dividend yield.

**Valuation Metrics:**
- Forward P/E: **18.48x** (vs. networking sector avg ~22.87x)
- P/S: ~4.0x
- Dividend Yield: ~3.2%
- EV/EBITDA: ~13x

**Growth Profile:**
- Revenue growth expected to re-accelerate in FY2027 as AI networking refresh hits
- Software subscription revenue mix now ~55% (improving earnings quality and recurring revenue)

**Competitive Moat:** Dominant enterprise network share (~50% of enterprise routing/switching); deep integration with enterprise IT stacks via IOS-XE and Catalyst Center.

**Key Risk:** Arista Networks continues to win AI fabric deployments in greenfield hyperscaler builds; Cisco's transformation to a software/subscription model still faces execution risk.

**Rating: Accumulate**

---

### 5. Salesforce (CRM) — Accumulate (Contrarian)

**Thesis:** At down 35% YTD and near 52-week lows, Salesforce now trades at valuation levels last seen during the 2022 rate shock. The market's thesis — that AI agents will disintermediate the CRM layer — is being tested by reality: enterprises running the Agentforce platform are using AI *on top of* Salesforce data, not instead of it. The installed base moat (150,000+ customers, petabytes of proprietary CRM data) makes Salesforce the most likely enterprise AI data layer, not a casualty of AI.

**Valuation Metrics:**
- Forward P/E: ~22x (vs. historical range of 35–60x; near trough)
- P/S: ~6x
- EV/EBITDA: ~18x
- PEG Ratio: ~1.2x (reasonable given 15–20% projected EPS growth)

**Growth Profile:**
- Revenue growth slowing to ~8–10% YoY as macro pressures seat count expansion
- However, AI-driven average revenue per user (ARPU) uplift via Agentforce premium pricing could reignite growth in FY2027

**Competitive Moat:** #1 global CRM by market share; Einstein AI and Agentforce are deeply integrated into Salesforce Flows and data architecture; switching costs are extremely high for enterprise customers.

**Key Risk:** Microsoft Dynamics + Copilot is the most credible competitive threat; if Microsoft can convert Copilot momentum into CRM seat wins, Salesforce's growth could deteriorate further before recovering.

**Rating: Accumulate** *(sizing: starter position; add on confirmation of Agentforce traction in Q2 print)*

## Risk Factors

Ranked by severity and immediacy:

1. **Strait of Hormuz Remains Closed / Iran Deal Collapses (CRITICAL):** If the reported US-Iran ceasefire extension fails to materialize, Brent crude could re-test $115–120/bbl, reigniting the inflation spiral and pushing May's 4.2% CPI print higher. This would kill any near-term rate cut narrative and could force the Fed to consider hikes. The entire Thursday rally could reverse in a single session.

2. **Persistent Above-Target Inflation Forces Fed Hawkishness (HIGH):** May CPI at 4.2% YoY is materially above the 2% target. If June CPI prints similarly hot, the market will begin pricing in rate hikes rather than cuts. A repricing of the terminal rate from 3.75% to 4.25%+ would be a significant valuation headwind, particularly for high-multiple growth stocks.

3. **AI Capex Demand Disappointment / Broadcom Guidance Signal (MEDIUM-HIGH):** Broadcom's Q3 AI chip sales guidance of $16B (vs. $17.2B expected) was the first sign of possible near-term demand softness. If NVIDIA's next earnings print or hyperscaler Q2 capex disclosures suggest any slowdown in GPU/ASIC orders, the AI trade — which is carrying the bulk of the S&P 500's YTD gain — could unwind sharply.

4. **SpaceX IPO Liquidity Drain (MEDIUM):** The $75B SpaceX offering is drawing institutional capital into a single, non-dividend-paying, cash-burning entity with an operating loss of $4.2B. The IPO lock-up mechanics and index inclusion timeline are uncertain. In the near term, the sheer size of the offering may divert capital from existing tech positions.

5. **China Export Control Escalation (MEDIUM):** Any new or tightened semiconductor export restrictions targeting China — which remains a significant end market for Intel, AMD, AMAT, and TSMC — could trigger another round of sector repricing similar to the October 2022 shock. NVIDIA's Vera CPU China outreach is walking a regulatory tightrope.

## Bottom Line

Thursday's broad-based rally was legitimate — Iran-driven oil relief, software sector momentum, and SpaceX IPO euphoria are real tailwinds — but the macro ceiling remains firmly in place: 4.2% inflation means the Fed is stuck, and the structural Hormuz disruption makes that inflation durable unless a formal deal closes. The highest-conviction trade entering the second half of 2026 is quality AI infrastructure at reasonable valuations: TSMC, Micron, and Marvell offer the best combinations of earnings power and valuation discipline in the sector, while Salesforce represents the most compelling deep-value contrarian opportunity for patient investors willing to hold through AI disruption noise.
